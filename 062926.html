Happy Monday, all.

Last week’s throughline was venue — who decides. This week’s is the fine print of the decision itself. Once a project clears the yes-or-no, the fight doesn’t end; it moves to the conditions stapled to the permit, and that is where the next eighteen months of contested builds will be won or lost. Our deep dive is the cleanest illustration we’ve seen of it: in New York, a state siting board — the same kind of state-level forum that, as we noted Friday, usually tilts toward developers — refused to let a 340-MW wind project loosen the nighttime-noise limit baked into its approval. The project lives. The condition lives harder. And the condition is the whole ballgame.

The pattern repeats down the stack. In Boulevard, California, a storage-plus-solar project is stalling not on its merits but on a fire-evacuation argument about the road out of town. In St. Armand, New York, a town wrote the condition as a flat ban. The lesson for our clients is that the design standards — decibels, setbacks, fire response, decommissioning bonds — are no longer the boring back half of the application. They are the front line, they are sticky once set, and the opposition has learned to litigate them long after the headline vote. Meanwhile the counter-fact holds: capital still closes on the projects that got the conditions right. A roughly $2.6 billion construction package landed on an Arizona solar-and-storage complex this week, and a 201-MW Texas solar farm broke ground. The build hasn’t stopped. It’s just gotten more conditional.

Six standing items, so we don’t re-litigate them. New York’s first-in-the-nation statewide data-center moratorium: still unsigned on Gov. Hochul’s desk — no signature, no veto. The Illinois POWER Act: no update. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: still no ruling on the reconsideration petition. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand. Sangamon County’s buffer-rezoning gambit: still no dated, in-window outcome, so we keep holding it. And Washington’s storage-moratorium wave: still widening, but no new in-window vote we hadn’t already logged. If any breaks, you’ll get a full item.

Must Read

Forty Decibels, Held

The most important word in a contested permit isn’t “approved.” It’s whatever comes after it. This week New York’s siting board proved the point on the loudest possible canvas. The state Board on Electric Generation Siting and the Environment unanimously denied a petition by Alle-Catt Wind Energy to remove the 40-dBA nighttime-noise design goal that protects non-participating neighbors of its 340-MW project — the standard that caps how loud the turbines can be at night for the people getting no lease check. Alle-Catt, a division of Chicago-based Invenergy, had swapped in larger turbines (tip height nearly 600 feet) to hit its 340 megawatts with fewer towers. Fewer towers, but louder ones — and the company asked the state to relax the very limit those bigger machines made hard to meet. The board said no, and said why: removing the protection “with no replacement” would “pose a public health risk.”

What makes this a teaching case isn’t the noise math; it’s the timing and the forum. The turbines are already going up — blades are being raised in Allegany and Wyoming counties as we write — so this is not a siting fight in the abstract. It is a developer, mid-construction, discovering that the condition it agreed to at approval is now a wall it has to build to. And it lost the request at the state level, the venue Friday’s issue flagged as the developer’s friendlier room. The same body that can override a town’s no can also refuse to soften a yes. The opposition that won it wasn’t a mob; it was a tidy coalition of two Cattaraugus County legislators (Ginger Schroder and Brenda Hanson), a single well-prepared resident (Stephanie Milks, who put sleep-disruption science on the record), and the Town of Freedom with Concerned Citizens, represented by environmental attorney Gary Abraham. They didn’t argue the project shouldn’t exist. They argued the condition should stay exactly as written — and the board agreed that long-term nighttime exposure is a different, more serious thing than the occasional spike.

That is the move our clients should expect to face on every build from here: not “stop the project,” but “hold the developer to the letter of the permit, forever.” It is a smarter play than a blanket no, because it survives the override regimes — Michigan’s PA 233, New York’s Article VIII, the state siting boards spreading across our 23 states — that were designed to take the veto away from towns. A community can lose the question of whether and still win the question of how, and “how” is where the cost, the schedule, and the operating headaches live. Hanson has already asked the Public Service Commission to set up a tracking mechanism for whether Alle-Catt actually stays under 40 dBA once the blades spin. The fight didn’t end at approval. It moved into the next decade.

The Takeaway

Treat the permit conditions as the real negotiation, not the paperwork after it. By the time a board is voting yes, the decibel limits, setbacks, fire-response plans, and decommissioning terms are already written — and this week shows they don’t soften later, even at the state level, even mid-construction. The developers who stay out of trouble will be the ones who pressure-test every condition before they accept it: can we actually hold 40 dBA at night with this turbine, and if not, do we size down now or fight a losing amendment in three years? For RenUSA, the engagement value is in front-loading that honesty with the host community — bringing the conservative, enforceable, transparent noise model the Town of Freedom asked for to the table first, rather than letting opponents define it after the fact. A condition you can live with is a license to operate. A condition you have to relitigate is a campaign that never ends.

Also On Our Radar
  • The Build Still Banks: Enlight’s U.S. arm Clēnera locked in roughly $2.6 billion in construction financing this week for the five-project CO Bar Complex in Arizona — about 1.2 GW of solar paired with 4 GWh of storage under a 1-GW interconnection, a total spend approaching $3 billion, backed by a seven-bank syndicate including BNP Paribas, MUFG, and Wells Fargo. CO Bar 1–3 are already under construction, with commercial operation phased across the back half of 2027 into 2028. It’s the counter-fact to keep in front of the team while we track denials and moratoriums all week: the rural-host solar-plus-storage model still clears the hardest test there is, which is a project-finance committee writing a ten-figure check. Where the land, the queue position, and the host community line up, the capital is not the bottleneck — the conditions are.
  • Boulevard’s Brake: San Diego County’s Planning Commission pushed its decision on the Starlight Solar project to July 10, after a June town hall turned the hearing into a referendum on the road out of town. The project pairs roughly 588 acres of solar with 217 MW of lithium-ion storage in Boulevard, and the “No Starlight Solar” campaign — joined by Manzanita and Kumeyaay tribal leaders — is fighting it not on panels but on siting: a Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone sitting next to the community’s only paved evacuation route. It’s the same fire-and-egress argument that stalled storage across the Northwest, now landing in solar-friendly Southern California, and it’s aimed squarely at the battery half of the project. The tell for our storage clients: in wildfire country, the evacuation map is now a siting document, and the developer who doesn’t bring a credible emergency-response and egress plan to the first meeting has already lost the framing.
Project Updates
  • OHThe Ohio Power Siting Board denied a second rehearing for Crossroads Solar I on June 24 — Open Road Renewables’ roughly 94-MW, 700-acre project in Knox County — cementing an earlier denial driven by local opposition. At the same meeting the board approved a 260-sheep grazing plan for the Madison Fields Solar Project in Madison County. The split screen in a single sitting is the lesson: the contested project gets the door, the project that showed up with a working-lands plan gets the green light.
  • NYThe Adirondack town of St. Armand unanimously banned battery storage and passed a one-year solar moratorium on June 27, aimed at Carson Power’s proposed BESS at a Trudeau Road substation. Residents split — some wanted the outright ban, while 64 signatures urged a narrower law three months into the existing pause — but the board moved on fire and drinking-water fears. Note the twofer: storage gets banned outright, solar gets frozen, in one meeting.
  • KSReno County commissioners voted down a proposed ban on battery storage and data centers on June 22, even after a late-May petition gathered 875 signatures from residents near Buhler. Commissioners noted no BESS or data-center applications are actually pending, and one argued local rail traffic is a far bigger hazard than either use. A rare county that declined to slam the door before anyone knocked — worth a case study in how “no application yet” can be the argument that keeps the door open.
  • IATwo northwest Iowa counties are mid-ordinance on solar and storage: Cherokee County supervisors are drafting solar/BESS rules amid a live request to build near Aurelia — after a 4-1 vote earlier this year to pause battery-storage clusters — and Pocahontas County is holding hearings on an ordinance covering an roughly 850-acre solar farm proposed south of Fonda by a Japanese energy firm. Both are the quiet, pre-vote stage where the setbacks and decommissioning terms our clients live with actually get written.
  • TXThe Public Utility Commission of Texas approved rules letting ERCOT batch-process large-load interconnection requests of 75 MW and up, starting with a “Batch Zero,” as the grid operator tracks more than 438 GW of large-load requests — nearly 90% of it data centers. A transmission plan for the first batch is expected in fall 2027. The queue our clients’ solar and storage projects share is being re-architected around hyperscale load, and the cost-allocation fight underneath it is the one to watch.
  • ILCarbondale’s City Council voted 4-1 to enact a one-year data-center moratorium, freezing rezoning and permitting while the city writes zoning standards it never had. It follows a May community meeting dominated by water, electrical-demand, noise, and light-pollution concerns — the now-standard shape of a city buying time to define the use before it has to rule on one.
  • WVHundreds packed a June 23 town hall in Wheeling’s Warwood neighborhood over Silicon Energy’s planned data center at the former Centre Foundry site, a project phased toward roughly 100 MW. The flashpoint is state, not local: Del. Shawn Fluharty argues West Virginia’s HB 2014 strips local control by exempting 90-MW-plus “high-impact” data centers from local noise and zoning rules — the same preemption pattern reshaping where the renewable fights get decided too.
On the Frontlines
  • TXShovels in Swisher: Vesper Energy started construction on the 201-MW Nazareth Solar project in Swisher County, right next to its 600-MW Hornet Solar plant, with the power headed into ERCOT and commercial operation targeted for autumn 2027 on roughly $236 million in financing closed earlier this month. It’s the build-still-closes counter-fact in its purest form: where the land assembly, the interconnection, and the offtake already line up, the contested-permit drama elsewhere doesn’t stop the dirt from moving. The Panhandle keeps proving that a developer who solved siting before the headlines is a developer who pours concrete on schedule.
  • VATen Hours, Please: Dominion Energy Virginia issued a request for information for at least 4,000 MWh of long-duration storage — technologies that can run 10-plus hours at full nameplate — flowing from 2026 legislation that created the commonwealth’s first LDES requirement. Dominion was careful to say the RFI commits it to nothing, but the signal is the story: the regulated utility is now shopping the exact long-duration build our storage clients are positioned to deliver, and the procurement design that follows will set the terms for years. Worth getting in front of before the RFP locks the specs.
  • USQueue Check: a new Advanced Energy United report finds the seven big grid operators are making “significant progress” on FERC Order 2023 interconnection-queue reforms — SPP and CAISO rated “promising,” MISO “incomplete,” PJM and the Eastern ISOs “expected” — even if there’s no hard proof yet that requests actually clear faster. The warning buried in the praise matters to our side of the build: the one-time fast-track tracks in MISO, PJM, and SPP skew toward utility-affiliated, fossil-heavy resources, which means the renewable projects waiting in line could watch others cut ahead under the banner of “reform.” Progress, yes — but uneven, and not obviously in our clients’ favor.
