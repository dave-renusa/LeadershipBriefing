Good morning, team.

Last week the moratorium wave crested across four states in three days. This week it hits the statehouse. The most consequential data center fight of the spring isn't in a county commissioner meeting — it's in Springfield, where Illinois lawmakers have thirteen days left to pass the POWER Act before the General Assembly adjourns May 31. As of Friday, the bill hasn't moved past subject-matter hearings, Governor Pritzker's office has said almost nothing since February, and the ComEd territory alone has roughly 100 large-load projects queued up. Meanwhile, at the local level, Normal, Illinois votes on a six-month moratorium tonight — the same night Bloomington holds a nonvoting discussion on its own pause. One state. Two clocks.

In North Carolina, a different kind of clock: clean energy groups asked the Utilities Commission for an expedited ruling by tomorrow, May 19, on their challenge to Chair Bill Brawley's unilateral April 23 order freezing Duke Energy's solar procurement for all of 2026. The order appeared without public hearing, without the other four commissioners, and without the standard notice stakeholders say they're entitled to. The SELC, Vote Solar, and Sierra Club are arguing it on procedural grounds — and they want an answer this week. If the commission doesn't act by Tuesday, the 2026 solar pipeline in North Carolina stays frozen through whatever appeal timeline follows.

Those are the two fires burning hottest this week. The broader map is below.

Must Read

Springfield's Clock

The Illinois POWER Act is the most ambitious attempt by any state legislature to regulate data center siting this session — and it may die quietly at the end of May without the governor saying a word about it. Capitol News Illinois reported Thursday that advocates are publicly calling out Governor Pritzker for what they're describing as a "lack of engagement" on the bill since he mentioned data centers in his February State of the State address. He proposed that PJM require developers to provide their own power and suggested a two-year pause on the state's data center tax credits. Then, relative silence.

The POWER Act would require community benefits agreements, ban nondisclosure agreements between data centers and municipalities, mandate water-use disclosures, require environmental impact assessments, and — the most contested provision — make developers build their own renewable energy to power their facilities. The Data Center Coalition says some of those requirements are "not insurmountable" and has been asking for formal stakeholder negotiations all session. The governor's office has not arranged them. As of Tuesday's House Energy and Environment Committee hearing, some members said they were learning the bill's details for the first time.

The urgency is real: three new data center projects have been approved in Illinois since the bill was introduced in February — in Sangamon County, Joliet, and Yorkville — each over residents' objections. Commonwealth Edison says the northern Illinois territory already has nearly 100 large-load projects in its interconnection queue, enough to more than double the territory's energy demand by 2040. The POWER Act is the only bill in play that addresses that load at a statewide level. Without it, every fight stays local — which is how Naperville and Pekin held the line, but also how Sangamon County approved a campus on a 17-10 vote after, residents said, the public comment window closed almost before it opened.

The Republican lane is real too. Rep. Jed Davis (R-Yorkville) filed his own bill requiring public notice and allowing citizen referendum votes on proposed data centers, saying his constituents "felt silenced." He told Capitol News Illinois he's "right on the fence" on the POWER Act — and that Republican colleagues are hearing the same constituent pressure. The bipartisan appetite for something exists. Whether Pritzker decides to lead, follow, or get out of the way before May 31 will determine whether Illinois ships a statewide framework or hands the next twelve months of data center decisions back to individual county boards.

⚡ The Takeaway: Illinois's legislative window closes May 31. The POWER Act needs executive engagement it hasn't gotten. If it dies this session, every data center fight in the ComEd territory defaults to the local level — which means more communities negotiating blind, more NDAs, and more projects approved before opposition can organize. The engagement calendar in Illinois just got a hard deadline.
Also On Our Radar
  • The Buyout Map Expands: The Trump administration's offshore wind cancellation campaign now spans three projects totaling 8 GW of planned generation — enough to power more than 3 million homes. As of Sunday's analysis from The Conversation via The Invading Sea, the confirmed buyouts are TotalEnergies (Attentive Energy off New Jersey plus a South Carolina lease), Golden State Wind (California), and Bluepoint Wind (New Jersey and New York). New York announced a $300 million state port infrastructure grant in early 2026 to support offshore wind. New Jersey's Wind Port represents more than $600 million in port investment. Both states' supply chain investments now hang on projects that no longer exist. The California Energy Commission and members of Congress are investigating whether the Interior Department's Judgment Fund — intended for legal settlements — is even the right vehicle for these payments given there was no active litigation with TotalEnergies. The federal buyoff play is advanced. The regional economic fallout is just beginning to land.
  • Normal Tonight: The Normal, Illinois Town Council votes Monday night on a six-month data center moratorium, with Bloomington's council holding its own nonvoting discussion the same evening. Town staff say data centers present "distinct challenges and impacts" the town's land-use regulations aren't built to handle. Mayor Chris Koos noted Normal hasn't even received an inquiry from a data center operator — making this a preemptive move, not a reactive one. That posture is significant. Champaign County already passed a one-year moratorium in April. McLean County proposed stricter guidelines in May. Logan County approved a 90-day pause. Central Illinois is converging on a de facto moratorium patchwork just as the state-level fight over the POWER Act enters its final three weeks.
  • Tar Heel Tipping Point: Clean energy groups asked the NC Utilities Commission for an expedited ruling by May 19 on their challenge to Chair Bill Brawley's April 23 order freezing Duke Energy's 2026 solar procurement. The order froze the entire cycle without a public hearing, without the other four commissioners, and without stakeholder notice. The Southern Environmental Law Center, Vote Solar, and Sierra Club are pushing on procedural grounds. Duke had proposed only 770 MW in 2026; Brawley seized on a 1 GW shortfall to argue for no procurement at all. If the commission doesn't grant reconsideration this week, North Carolina's 2026 solar pipeline stays frozen — with Duke's next rate hike proposal already pending before the same body.
  • Imperial Recall: Calipatria, California's city council voted 4-0 on May 15 to hold public hearings for a data center moratorium — a move that would make it the first jurisdiction in Imperial County to enact one. The meeting's opening was set by residents serving recall notices on three elected officials: Mayor Michael Luellen, Mayor Pro-Tem Fred Beltran, and Council Member Jesse Rivas. Despite the looming recalls, the council maintained a "united front against aggressive data center companies." The recall-plus-moratorium simultaneity is a new pattern: opposition coalitions no longer waiting for election cycles but using recall mechanisms to create immediate political risk for officials seen as too accommodating. Imperial County is near the California-Arizona border — a geography that is drawing significant data center interest.
On the Frontlines
  • ILSpringfield's Last Mile: The Illinois General Assembly has thirteen days before its scheduled May 31 adjournment. The POWER Act is the primary vehicle for statewide data center regulation. As of Thursday, it hadn't moved past subject matter hearings since February. Governor Pritzker's office offered a statement about monitoring "fiscal impact" but no engagement signal. The bill would require community benefits agreements, ban NDAs with local governments, mandate water-use reports, and require developers to provide their own renewable power. Three data center projects in the state were approved after the bill was introduced. Watch for floor movement this week — or the absence of it.
  • NCSolar's Stay of Execution: The expedited reconsideration petition on NCUC Chair Brawley's unilateral solar freeze comes due Tuesday, May 19. The WFAE/Carolina Public Press reporting noted the order was unusual because Brawley appeared to author it alone, without the four other commissioners. Duke Energy's 2026 procurement cycle — 770 MW proposed — has been halted since April 23. SELC and allies argue this raises both procedural and substantive legal questions. A denial of reconsideration likely means litigation. The same NCUC is handling Duke's next rate case. The two proceedings will run in parallel, creating leverage dynamics on both sides of the docket.
  • NJBluepoint Bows Out: Bluepoint Wind, a joint venture of Engie and EDP Renewables' Ocean Winds, has accepted the federal buyout for its lease areas off New Jersey and New York. The New Jersey Wind Port — a state investment exceeding $600 million — was positioned to support Bluepoint's turbine assembly. New Jersey Governor Sherrill's office, which received the 60-organization emergency moratorium petition for data centers last week, now also faces questions about what the offshore wind infrastructure investment is worth without projects to support. Two major energy fights, two different industries, same political moment for one governor.
  • NYThe $300M Question: New York announced a $300 million state grant for offshore wind port infrastructure in early 2026. With Bluepoint Wind accepting the federal buyout, the lease area off New York that was supposed to anchor that port investment is now vacant. The California Energy Commission and members of Congress are investigating whether the Judgment Fund payments were even legal. New York's delegation is among those pushing back. Watch for a state-level response from Albany this week — the $300M figure is a clean political number and the governor will need to say something about it.
