RenUSA Leadership Brief

Volume 9 · Friday, May 29, 2026

Friday morning, team.

The week that closes with today’s brief began and ended at county boards — and county boards won. Wednesday night, four Hanover County supervisors voted 4–3 to kill a $900 million data center campus that the planning commission had recommended approving a month earlier. Across Central Virginia, Valley Link released revised transmission maps showing that sustained opposition had shrunk the proposed route through Goochland County from 28 miles of options down to 1.3. And in Rockford, a state legislator called the Illinois POWER Act “dead” with four days left in the spring session. The institutional level that moved the needle this week was not the statehouse. It was the county board.

The Hanover story is worth sitting with. Residents spent six months organizing: matching red shirts, “Grow Tomatoes, Not Data Centers” signs, packed planning commission meetings, and a cross-partisan constituency that one organizer described as “every spectrum of people.” The planning commission still recommended approval. Then the board overruled it, 4–3. Supervisor Faye Prichard’s explanation: “The reality is, I don’t think this many citizens come forward to say this is really something we don’t want, is something I have the ability to ignore because I work for you.” That sentence is the community-engagement brief. Sustained visible opposition, applied to the decision-maker who faces voters directly, produced a different outcome than the professional process was headed toward.

In North Carolina, the silence has now held for 37 days. Chair Brawley’s April 23 unilateral solar freeze is still on the NCUC docket. No reconsideration ruling. No commission response. The comment deadline passed May 22. Duke Energy’s entire 2026 solar procurement cycle — 770-plus megawatts — remains in administrative suspension. The longer the silence holds, the harder it becomes to call the freeze an anomaly. A ruling in either direction ends one phase of this fight. The absence of a ruling is its own kind of policy.

Also On Our Radar
  • The Mountain Road No: The Hanover County Board of Supervisors voted 4–3 Wednesday night to deny the Mountain Road Technology Park — a proposed 430-acre campus by Denver-based land-acquisition firm Tract that the developer said would generate roughly $900 million in county revenue over 20 years. Neighbors countered it would consume between 600,000 and 2 million gallons of water per day, emit persistent noise, and permanently alter a rural landscape. Tract had offered $15 million for county water infrastructure as a concession. The planning commission recommended approval in April anyway. A six-month constituent campaign, organized under the “Stop Hanover Data Centers” banner, reversed that recommendation at the supervisors’ level. Three supervisors voted yes. Four voted no. The deciding votes cited constituent volume, not a technical defect in the project. That is the outcome when you build the visible opposition before the final hearing rather than at it.
  • The Map That Moved: Valley Link released revised transmission line routes Wednesday showing its proposed corridor through Goochland County had shrunk from roughly 28 miles of options down to approximately 1.3 miles — a reduction the project team attributed to community input received over the previous three months. Goochland’s Board of Supervisors held a special meeting Thursday at the county high school auditorium to review the maps and press Valley Link representatives with questions on health impacts, construction timelines, and the project’s fundamental premise. Supervisor Jonathan Lyle asked directly: “If demand is in Northern Virginia, why are we not putting generation capacity up there?” A Valley Link spokesperson confirmed that “continued economic development, population growth, and of course the expansion of data centers across Virginia” is driving the project. At least seven of the nine affected county boards have passed opposition resolutions; three have allocated $250,000 from their budgets for potential legal battles. The State Corporation Commission will make the final siting decision; Valley Link estimates that ruling at the end of 2027.
  • Springfield Stalls Out: The Illinois POWER Act — 638 pages of data center regulation requiring community benefits agreements, water-use permits, and a ban on NDAs between developers and local governments — has not reached the floor of either chamber with four days left before the May 31 adjournment. State Rep. John Cabello (R-Machesney Park) said flatly: “Right now, it’s dead.” State Rep. Dave Vella (D-Rockford) offered that the bill was “a very good start” but warned against “rushing in too fast and making bad law.” Neither has signed on as a sponsor. The Illinois Environmental Council has a lobby day scheduled Saturday at the capitol — the same day the session ends. If the bill dies at adjournment, the next window is veto session in October. The local governments did not wait: Normal’s six-month moratorium is running, Denver has its one-year freeze, and Dubuque County is now one week into its own twelve-month pause. The legislature is catching up to what the counties already decided.
  • Iowa’s Twelve-Month Line: Dubuque County’s twelve-month data center moratorium is now one week old — passed 2–1 at a special meeting where at least 250 people attended in person and virtually, and where a supervisor moved from the floor to expand the proposed 120-day pause to a full year before the meeting was ten minutes old. A developer had already contacted the city of Dubuque about water and sewer service for a proposed site south of the city limits; the city confirmed the contact but said no annexation petition had been filed. County staff now have twelve months to draft zoning regulations, with public town halls to be scheduled. The county that just said no has become a planning exercise. That is what the opposition wants: time and rules, in that order.
  • NC: 37 Days Without a Ruling: The comment deadline on the reconsideration petition for Chair Brawley’s unilateral solar freeze passed May 22. As of this morning, no ruling has appeared on the North Carolina Utilities Commission public docket. Brawley’s April 23 order — issued without the other four commissioners, without notice, and without an evidentiary record — effectively cancelled Duke Energy’s entire 2026 solar procurement cycle. The Carolinas Clean Energy Business Association, the Southern Environmental Law Center, Vote Solar, the Sierra Club, and others petitioned for reconsideration. The commission has been silent. Every day of silence is another day closer to a permanent outcome. If reconsideration is denied, litigation is the next move. At some point the silence stops being a procedural gap and starts being an answer.
On the Frontlines
  • VAThe Hanover Line: Four supervisors voted no on the Mountain Road Technology Park Wednesday night, overruling the planning commission’s April recommendation of approval. The project — 430 acres, three substations, up to 2 million gallons of water per day — had been contested for six months. The deciding supervisors cited constituent volume. The planning commission had already said yes. The board said no anyway. That sequencing matters: a project that clears the professional process can still lose at the elected level if the constituent record is strong enough.
  • VAValley Link Retreats the Map: Valley Link’s Wednesday route release showed Goochland County’s proposed corridor shrunk from 28 miles to 1.3 following community pressure campaigns across nine counties. The Goochland board held a special session Thursday. Residents who had organized and packed county meetings heard a Valley Link representative confirm that “citizen involvement matters” — and saw the map to prove it. The SCC siting ruling is still more than a year away. June open houses are scheduled in all nine counties.
  • ILPOWER Act in the Drawer: The POWER Act has not reached the floor with four days left in the spring session. A Republican called it dead; a Democrat called it a good start but not ready. An Environmental Council lobby day Saturday is the last push. If the bill dies at adjournment, veto session in October is the next window. What the bill would have done — ban NDAs, require community benefits agreements, mandate water-use permits — local governments are doing piecemeal without it.
  • IADubuque’s First Week: The twelve-month moratorium passed last Tuesday is now the governing framework for data center siting in Iowa’s most densely organized county opposition. A developer had already been in contact with the city before the vote; that contact did not change the outcome. County staff now have a year to draft rules. The planning and zoning commission had unanimously recommended the pause the week prior — the elected board amplified it fourfold.
  • NCThe Docket Stays Empty: Thirty-seven days since Chair Brawley’s April 23 unilateral solar freeze and counting. The comment deadline passed May 22. No ruling has appeared on the NCUC public docket. Duke Energy’s 2026 procurement cycle — 770-plus megawatts — remains frozen. The original petition for reconsideration came from the Carolinas Clean Energy Business Association, arguing Brawley lacked authority to act unilaterally. The rest of the commission has not publicly agreed or disagreed. If reconsideration is denied, the courts are next. If the commission never rules, the freeze holds by default until the 2026 carbon plan is approved — which is not guaranteed before year-end.
  • OHOak Run Still Remanded: The Ohio Supreme Court’s Tuesday ruling vacating the Power Siting Board’s approval of Oak Run Solar in Madison County — covered at length in Wednesday’s brief — has produced no immediate response from the developer or the board. The 800-megawatt, 6,000-acre project now restarts administrative proceedings with no timeline set. The ruling adds visual-impact documentation requirements on top of an already multi-layered opposition process. No new action is expected before summer.
Plays of the Week
  • Stop Hanover Data CentersThe Six-Month Grow: The coalition that killed the Mountain Road Tech Park didn’t build in the last week before the vote — it built continuously for six months. Matching red shirts. A “Grow Tomatoes, Not Data Centers” brand simple enough to fit on a lawn sign and sharp enough to get quoted in every news story. Rallies outside the meeting before speakers went inside. And critically: a cross-partisan constituency that made opposition socially visible across every political lane in the county. The planning commission still recommended approval. The board overruled it. The gap between those two outcomes is exactly what six months of constituent presence produced. The lesson for any contested project: the day the planning commission votes is not when the constituent record is built. It’s when you find out whether you built it.
  • Goochland Board of SupervisorsThe Multi-County Map Fight: Goochland didn’t fight Valley Link alone. Seven county boards passed opposition resolutions; three allocated $250,000 for legal fees. The coordinated multi-jurisdictional posture created a legal and political threat across the entire 115-mile corridor, not just one county’s slice of it. Valley Link had to respond to a coalition, not an outlier. The result: Goochland’s exposure went from 28 miles of options to 1.3. That reduction didn’t come from a single board meeting — it came from months of organized presence at every county’s open houses, board meetings, and planning sessions. For transmission projects that cross multiple jurisdictions, the multi-county coalition that allocates real legal money is a different kind of adversary than a single township in the corridor.
  • Supervisor Ann McDonough, Dubuque CountyThe Floor Amendment: When Dubuque County’s special meeting convened to consider a 120-day data center pause, Supervisor McDonough moved within the first ten minutes to expand it to twelve months. “I do not support data centers and I have said that in the past repeatedly,” she said. “This piece of paper says four months. It should be one year minimum.” Chair Pothoff joined her. The vote was 2–1. The duration quadrupled before the staff presentation was over. The play: if you have the votes and you have a prepared motion, don’t accept the terms on the table. A 120-day pause gives a developer room to run out the clock and re-apply. A twelve-month moratorium gives staff time to write real rules. McDonough knew the difference, came prepared to make the motion, and executed it. Opposition wins are often decided by who prepared for the meeting more thoroughly.
