Good morning, team.

The renewable build is getting squeezed from both ends at once, and Wednesday is the day we look at the squeeze head‑on. At the bottom of the system, townships and county commissions keep saying no even where the state has taken the siting pen out of their hands: a Hillsdale County board killed a 140‑megawatt solar farm Monday night, an Arizona commission just recommended killing a 400‑megawatt battery project, and a Virginia county is sprouting “No Industrial Solar” signs ahead of its own planning hearing. At the top, the federal government has quietly frozen the wind pipeline, stranding roughly $2 billion of projects in our own footprint without a single public vote. For a firm whose work lives in the gap between a developer’s plan and a community’s permission, the lesson of the week is that the permission problem now comes from two directions, and the one nobody campaigned against is the one doing the most damage.

Four standing items, so we don’t re‑litigate them. The New York statewide data‑center moratorium: no update, still unsigned on Gov. Hochul’s desk, and she has until December to act, so the veto stays live. The Illinois POWER Act: no update, summer negotiations continue. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: still no ruling on the reconsideration petition. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand, no timeline set. If any breaks, you’ll get a full item.

The longer look this morning is Michigan, where the state spent two years building the power to overrule local boards on clean energy, and the local boards keep winning anyway.

Must Read

Override on Paper, Veto in the Room

Applause broke out in a Hillsdale County meeting room Monday night when the Fayette Township Planning Commission voted 4‑0 to deny Ranger Power’s proposed 140‑megawatt solar farm. Two weeks earlier, more than ninety people had surrounded the same developer’s director of development at a canceled Wexford County meeting over a separate 1,400‑acre proposal. The scenes capture the contradiction at the center of Michigan’s clean‑energy build: since 2023, state law has let renewable developers bypass local governments entirely and seek approval from the Michigan Public Service Commission, and yet developers keep losing on the ground anyway.

The numbers tell the story. Michigan has at least 64 large‑scale solar projects and 42 wind projects operating today, but the Public Service Commission is now sitting on ten active siting cases, three of them filed by Ranger Power alone, that were either rejected locally or ran into organized opposition. A May appeals‑court ruling cleared the way for the commission to keep overriding local denials, so the legal preemption is real. What the preemption cannot do is make the room friendly. Developers still need a documented history of local outreach to use the state path, and townships can still intervene in the state proceeding, which means the community fight does not end when the developer goes to Lansing. It just moves venue and adds a year.

The engagement failure underneath all of this is worth naming, because a Michigan State University bioenergy educator named it bluntly: solar developers, he said, “don’t lead with agriculture, they lead with megawatts.” Ranger Power has poured more than $500,000 into Michigan community foundations and school programs, and it still watched the Hillsdale County Community Foundation hand the money back rather than be seen as taking the developer’s side. Checks did not buy consent. Meanwhile the politics are hardening above the local level too: Michigan House Republicans passed bills in May to repeal the 2040 clean‑energy mandate outright, dead on arrival in the Senate this year but a marker for the next election. The throughline is that money spent late and framed as megawatts does not move a room that has already decided the project is something being done to it.

The Takeaway

State preemption is not the same thing as community consent, and Michigan is the cleanest proof we have. A developer can win the legal right to override a township and still lose two years and a reputation in the process, because the override statute says nothing about whether the neighbors trust you. Two lessons for our work. First, the outreach has to be real and early enough to satisfy the state’s own “history of engagement” test, not a check written the month before the hearing; a foundation that returns your donation is worse than one you never funded. Second, lead with the land. The projects that survive in farm country are the ones that show up talking about grazing, crop rotation, and agrivoltaics before they talk about capacity, because the fight is rarely about electrons and almost always about what the place is going to look like and who got to decide.

Also On Our Radar
  • Muddy Creek, Take Two: Hanwha’s Qcells unit has filed a preliminary application with the Oregon Energy Facility Siting Council for the Muddy Creek Energy Park, a 150‑megawatt solar project with battery storage spread across roughly 800 acres of Linn County farmland east of Interstate 5. The developer is leaning hard on the agrivoltaics frame, pledging that more than 700 of the acres will stay in production with sheep grazing under the panels, and one leasing landowner is already running that playbook on other Western Oregon solar sites. The opposition group Friends of Gap Road is not sold, warning about wetland habitat and lost farmland and arguing the company should build on a warehouse roof or a parking lot instead. Watch this one as a test of whether “keep farming it” actually defuses a siting fight or just reframes it: Oregon is in our footprint, and the state‑council path here echoes the Michigan dynamic in today’s Must Read, where the regulator can approve but the neighbors still set the terms of the argument.
  • The Wind Freeze Nobody Voted For: Billions of dollars in wind projects across Illinois are stalled, including five in McLean, Peoria, and Tazewell counties, after the Defense Department quietly stopped clearing turbine projects through the FAA review they need to proceed. A union official pegged the Illinois hit alone at roughly $2 billion in private investment, 800 turbines, and 2,849 megawatts, with one Knox County project already dead rather than merely delayed. The mechanism is bureaucratic rather than legislative: easy cases that used to clear in a day or two stopped getting certificates in late April, and renewable advocates count 250 projects across 30 states now frozen, most of them in red rural areas. The point for our work is that this is a permitting bottleneck with no hearing, no comment period, and no local board to persuade, which makes it the rare project killer that community engagement cannot touch, and a reminder to clients that the entitlement risk on wind now sits as much in a federal clearinghouse as in any township.
Project Updates
  • WASeattle did it: the City Council voted unanimously Tuesday to impose a one‑year moratorium on large data centers, making it the largest U.S. city to freeze the sector. The ordinance covers facilities above 20 megavolt‑amperes, takes effect immediately under an emergency designation, can be extended six months, and pairs with a resolution directing departments to study grid, water, rate, and public‑health impacts. Mayor Katie Wilson says she will sign it. The trigger was concrete: four companies had approached Seattle City Light about five large‑scale centers totaling 369 megawatts. This closes out the preview we ran Monday.
  • NCCharlotte’s City Council approved its 150‑day data‑center moratorium Monday night, 11‑0, the vote we flagged on the docket. The pause runs through Nov. 5 while staff draft new rules and does not touch projects already under construction. Council member Victoria Watlington framed it as buying time to “agree on the facts” before writing policy, after residents raised the now‑standard trio of complaints: power bills, water use, and noise. With both Charlotte and Seattle landing yes votes inside twenty‑four hours, the big‑city pause is now a national pattern, not a coastal one.
  • AZNextEra’s 400‑megawatt Valley Farms battery project near Coolidge is heading to the Pinal County Board of Supervisors carrying a planning‑commission recommendation of denial, 6‑3, per a June 9 account in the Pinal Post. This is NextEra’s second swing at the site; the company stripped the solar component, shrank the footprint to under 69 acres, and added a buffer wall after an earlier version failed. It still drew opposition from the City of Coolidge, which says the site conflicts with its general plan, and from neighbors citing battery‑fire and well‑contamination fears, several invoking the Moss Landing and Otay Mesa fires by name. The supervisors are not bound by the recommendation, and NextEra has promised another neighborhood meeting before that vote, which is the window that decides this one.

On the Frontlines
  • VAKeep Botetourt Green: Botetourt County, in the Roanoke Valley, has sprouted “No Industrial Solar” signs ahead of a planning‑commission hearing on two utility‑scale solar farms that would be the largest in the region, even as a Google data center moves through the same county on already‑zoned land. The opposition rallying cry is “Keep Botetourt Green,” and the tracts in question are a 53‑acre hillside on U.S. 220 and a 21‑acre parcel behind a row of houses, neither obviously visible from the road, both arguably destined for subdivision if not solar. The engagement read is the one we keep flagging: this is a viewshed fight dressed as a land‑use fight, in a county that voted overwhelmingly Republican and is now opposing both the solar its own utility needs to meet the Virginia Clean Economy Act and the data center the national party champions. Any solar client west of the Blue Ridge should expect the “industrial” framing and the property‑rights contradiction to travel, because the signs already are.
