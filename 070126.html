Good morning, team.

Monday’s lesson was that the permit condition, not the vote, is the real project. Today’s deep dive is the sequel: when a home-rule town can’t or won’t write those conditions yet, it does one of two things — and this week two New York towns did both, in the same 48 hours, on the same technology. In Chautauqua County, the Town of Busti froze solar, wind, and battery storage all at once, extending three separate moratoriums to mid-2027 while it waits on state guidance. Ninety minutes east, the Town of Clay did the opposite: it wrote the storage rulebook and adopted it, keeping live battery applications moving through the process. Freeze it or zone it. That is the fork every one of our storage and solar clients now hits in these states, and which road a town takes is decided months before anyone files.

The counter-fact holds, and it’s louder than the moratoriums: the money is not waiting for anyone. Wood Mackenzie this week put the U.S. storage market on track to roughly quadruple to 200 GW by 2031, off a record first quarter. A residential fleet the size of a fossil plant got pointed straight at the data-center cost fight. Tax equity closed on a 480-MWh Texas battery and a 381-MWh Michigan one in the same news cycle. The build doesn’t stop when a town votes no. It just relocates to the town that voted how.

Must Read

Freeze It or Zone It

Two New York towns, one week, one technology, opposite verbs. It is the cleanest side-by-side we’ve seen of the choice facing every community our clients build in — and of why the choice is made long before a shovel is in the ground. On Monday, the Town of Busti in Chautauqua County held three public hearings and, hearing not a single comment from the public, approved three separate moratorium laws in a row: Local Law 1 extends the town’s pause on commercial solar to June 30, 2027; Local Law 2 does the same for commercial wind; Local Law 3 does it for battery energy storage — the storage freeze explicitly held open until a NYSERDA working group finishes studying such facilities and the town rewrites its land-use rules. One meeting, three sectors, everything on ice for a year. The next day, the reporting landed on the Town of Clay in Onondaga County, which took the other road entirely: it adopted a new Battery Energy Storage System Law — Chapter 209 of the town code — setting the siting, design, operation, and maintenance standards for storage, and cleared it with a negative environmental declaration so projects can proceed under a known rulebook. Clay paused data centers; it did not pause storage. It regulated it.

Read the two together and the strategic point writes itself. A moratorium and an ordinance are not opposites in spirit — both are a town saying “we don’t have rules yet” — but they are opposites in what they do to a development timeline. Busti’s freeze is a soft veto with a clean alibi: nobody has to argue the merits of any specific project, the town simply declines to consider any of them for twelve months and points at Albany. It is defensible, it is quiet (no public even showed up to object), and it is sticky, because “we’re waiting on the state” renews as easily as it passes. Clay’s ordinance is the harder, better civic act: it forces the town to decide, in writing and in public, what a good battery project looks like — where it goes, how far from homes, what the emergency-response plan has to contain — and then it lets the applications that meet the bar go forward. Clay had real projects in front of it when it did this, including battery proposals routed through its Planning Board; rather than stop the clock, it built the track they run on.

For RenUSA, the lesson is about where we spend the engagement dollar, and when. The temptation is to fight the freeze — to show up at the moratorium hearing and argue it’s premature. Busti proves how badly that misreads the room: the freeze passed to an empty gallery, because a study period feels reasonable to everyone and threatening to no one. The higher-leverage play is upstream and constructive — be the party that hands the town the Chapter 209 it doesn’t have the staff to write. The conservative setback table, the fire-response protocol keyed to the local department, the decommissioning bond language, the NYSERDA-model-law crosswalk — the towns that end up like Clay are the ones someone helped get there, and the towns that end up like Busti are the ones left to wait because writing the rulebook alone felt harder than pressing pause. A freeze you could have prevented with a model ordinance is the most expensive year in a project’s life.

The Takeaway

When a home-rule town says it has “no rules yet” for storage, it is about to either freeze the sector or zone it — and the difference is roughly a year of schedule. Busti froze solar, wind, and BESS together to an empty hearing room; Clay wrote a storage ordinance and kept its battery applications alive. The winning move for our clients isn’t to contest the moratorium after it’s filed — it’s to arrive early with a credible, host-community-tuned ordinance so the town has a road that isn’t “wait until 2027.” Bring the rulebook, or inherit the freeze.

Also On Our Radar
  • The Grid in the Garage: Sunrun, Tesla, and Renew Home unveiled a framework to aggregate more than 16 GW of home batteries and smart thermostats into what they’re calling the country’s largest virtual power plant, announced June 24 and pointed squarely at the fight our clients keep getting dragged into — who pays for the data-center load. The pitch: hundreds of thousands of residential batteries plus eight million-plus connected devices, needing no new land, water, or interconnection, dispatched to shave the exact peaks that are driving the transmission build-out. They report more than 300 MW ready to deploy immediately in Virginia’s “Data Center Alley,” a commitment into PJM’s proposed reliability backstop that could unlock over a gigawatt, and a Brattle Group estimate that squeezing existing grid capacity harder could cut U.S. bills $110–170 billion over a decade. The tell for us: the “ratepayers shouldn’t foot the bill for hyperscalers” argument — the one opponents use to kill projects — is now being wielded for distributed renewables. That’s a message our clients can borrow.
  • The Money Compounds: Wood Mackenzie’s Q2 U.S. Energy Storage Monitor, out June 23, projects the market roughly quadrupling to 200 GW / 655 GWh of cumulative installed capacity by 2031, on the back of a record first quarter — 3.3 GW / 8.4 GWh installed in Q1 2026, with utility-scale alone posting 2.3 GW / 6.8 GWh. Texas, California, and Arizona still lead, but the report flags Michigan and Georgia gaining momentum and calls New York and Illinois the fastest-growing markets ahead — the same states stacking up town-level moratoriums. Near-term growth is throttled by safe-harbored inventory and the scramble for FEOC-compliant systems, not by demand. Keep this in front of the team on the slow news days: the sector our clients build in is on a four-x trajectory, and the binding constraint is siting and supply, not appetite.
Project Updates
  • TXOCI Energy closed roughly $130 million in tax equity for its 120-MW / 480-MWh Alamo City BESS in Bexar County, structured with Greenprint Capital under the technology-neutral Clean Electricity Investment Credit and tied to a long-term capacity agreement with CPS Energy, the nation’s largest municipally owned utility. Announced June 22, the project’s four-hour duration puts it in a small club of long-duration assets in an ERCOT market where most batteries run about two hours — ING on construction debt, LG Energy Solution Vertech on cells, Elgin Power on EPC, commercial operation targeted for 2027. San Antonio’s muni buying four-hour storage under contract is the quiet template: when the offtaker is the city itself, the siting fight mostly evaporates.
  • MICanadian Solar’s e-Storage will supply a 75-MW / 381-MWh battery to Apex Clean Energy in Branch County, co-located with Apex’s operating Coldwater Solar facility, announced June 24, with deliveries starting early 2027 and commercial operation targeted for mid-2027. The backdrop is the pressure our Michigan clients are riding: state law requires utilities to bring 2,500 MW of storage online by 2030 while the state’s largest coal units retire through 2032, pulling dispatchable capacity off the MISO grid. Pairing new storage onto an already-permitted solar site is the path of least resistance — the land, the interconnection, and the community relationship are already there.
  • PAMuncy Creek Township supervisors granted conditional-use approval June 25 for Bollinger Solar’s roughly 32-MW, 52,000-panel array, paired with a hybrid egg-laying operation, after fifteen months of periodic hearings in Lycoming County. The approval came loaded: forty conditions, the barn count cut from five to three, and the solar kept out of the residential zone entirely, with a development-plan review still ahead. It’s Monday’s thesis in miniature — the “yes” and the forty strings attached to it were negotiated in the same breath, and the forty strings are where the next two years of this project actually live.

On the Frontlines
  • ORBuy the Room With Daycare: Avangrid announced a 41-MW / 82-MWh battery in Gilliam County, the Shutler Energy Storage project, on June 24, and the engineering is the least interesting part. The community package is the case study: about 35 union construction jobs, and $110,000 a year for the life of the project split evenly between the Condon Early Learning Centre and the Arlington Childcare Centre — because the county judge told the developer that childcare cost and availability are the real barrier to keeping a workforce in a rural county. That is the host-community benefit done right: not a generic check to the general fund, but money aimed at the specific thing the community says it’s short on, framed by a local official as a partnership. For our storage clients staring down the fire-and-safety narrative, this is the counter-narrative — show up solving a problem the town already has.
  • CAThe Public Option Out-Greens the Utility: California’s Community Choice Aggregators keep outpacing the big investor-owned utilities on storage and clean procurement, with two fresh moves this week: the 4-MW / 16-MWh Redemeyer Road solar-plus-storage project in Ukiah for Sonoma Clean Power (ribbon-cutting July 14), and a Central Coast Community Energy–Lunar Energy virtual power plant, announced June 24, that will orchestrate customer home batteries starting around 5 MW and scaling toward 30 MW by 2030. The number worth carrying into a skeptical town-board room: CCAs delivered 73% renewable power to customers in 2024, more than double the 35% from the IOUs in the same territory. Where the buyer is a locally controlled public agency, the clean build isn’t a fight the developer has to win — it’s the agency’s stated mission, and the storage rides in on it.
