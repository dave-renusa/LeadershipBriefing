RenUSA Leadership Brief

Volume 6 · Friday, May 22, 2026

Friday morning, team.

The opposition stopped waiting for state capitols. This week it went straight to city hall — and city hall said yes. Denver’s council voted unanimously Monday to freeze new data center construction for a year. Normal, Illinois voted the same way Monday night. Seattle’s council is days away from its own vote. In Texas, Hill County is holding a moratorium despite immediate lawsuit threats, and Somervell County passed a formal resolution opposing construction until the legislature acts. The political move that once took organized opposition months to execute at the statehouse now takes a single council meeting.

What’s changed is not the opposition’s intensity — that has been building for months. What’s changed is the target. State-level moratoriums require legislative majorities, survive governors, and take time. Municipal and county moratoriums require a quorum, a staff memo, and a determined constituent base. Normal’s vote was unanimous. Denver’s was unanimous. The threshold is lower and the feedback loop is faster: a concerned farmer shows up, a water-use chart circulates, and by the next agenda cycle the planning staff has a memo recommending a pause. That is the same sequence that has blocked solar and wind for years. It is now running in parallel on data centers in every state where the team operates.

In Texas, the political topology looks identical to the rural solar fights RenUSA has navigated: a state government firmly in the developers’ corner, county officials facing constituent fury, and a legal framework that makes moratoriums legally questionable but politically unavoidable. Hood County residents sued a Bitcoin data center for noise-induced headaches and tinnitus. Someone literally prayed at a Granbury city council meeting that a rezoning vote would fail. The prayer didn’t work. The lawsuit might. When opposition reaches the prayer-and-lawsuit stage in a conservative Texas exurb, the engagement environment has fundamentally changed. That was true for solar five years ago in rural communities. It’s true for data centers today everywhere.

Also On Our Radar
  • The Mile-High Mandate: Denver’s city council voted unanimously Monday to impose a one-year moratorium on new data center construction, effective May 21 — the first major U.S. city to do so. The moratorium halts processing of new zoning permits and site development plans while Denver drafts energy, water, and noise standards it currently has none of. The CoreSite DE3 facility under construction at 49th and Race is exempted, but the two additional buildings CoreSite planned for that campus are frozen. Denver Water had estimated that single facility at roughly 235,000 gallons per day, or 86 million gallons per year. The vote was unanimous at every stage. That is not moderation — that is a constituency problem so severe that no council member wanted to be on the wrong side of it.
  • Seattle’s One-Year Window: Seattle City Council members introduced legislation May 15 for a one-year moratorium on large data centers while the city develops energy, water, and noise regulations it does not currently have. Four companies had proposed five facilities drawing a combined 369 megawatts — roughly one-third of the city’s average daily power use. Two developers have already withdrawn their proposals. Councilmember Eddie Lin is sponsoring the measure; Council President Joy Hollingsworth called it “not a knee-jerk reaction.” A June 3 committee vote is scheduled. Mayor Katie Wilson’s administration is developing large-load policies for Seattle City Light in parallel. The gap between “we don’t have rules” and “we have a moratorium” is now measured in weeks.
  • Normal’s Preemptive Strike: Normal, Illinois approved a six-month data center moratorium through November 30 at its May 18 council meeting — despite the town having received zero data center inquiries. Mayor Chris Koos acknowledged no operator had approached the city, but staff recommended the pause to “study best practices, evaluate local conditions, and develop clear, consistent standards.” Normal is acting preemptively, before any project lands, specifically because it watched what happened in other Illinois communities when they didn’t. That is a new posture. Local governments are now building the moat before the developer shows up.
  • The Smart Siting Play: A Cornell University study published Monday found that restricting utility-scale solar to avoid ecologically sensitive lands and prime farmland adds just 0.17% to annualized system costs — essentially rounding error. The research, conducted with The Nature Conservancy and the U.S. Geological Survey using 46,000 MW of New York deployment as the case study, shows that developers who run biodiversity-first macro-siting models can bring a planning board a map proving they already protected the land that opposition will cite. The fight becomes much harder to have when the developer neutralized the argument before submitting. The cost of smart siting is smaller than the cost of any single contested docket.
On the Frontlines
  • CODenver Goes First: The moratorium took effect Thursday. A working group will assess impacts and draft standards while the freeze holds. Denver had no zoning rules specific to data centers, no energy requirements, and no water-use standards — which is precisely why the council felt it had no choice but to pause. Every major city facing a large-load proposal is now watching Denver’s working group model.
  • WASeattle’s Clock: A June 3 committee vote is scheduled. Two of four developers have already withdrawn proposals. The Chamber of Commerce objects — its president told Axios restricting data centers is “more symbolic than functional” — but the council appears unmoved. When the council president and two other members co-sponsor the same measure, symbolism and policy have converged.
  • ORHillsboro’s Line: Portland DSA organized a 60-person rally May 16 near current and planned data center construction in Hillsboro, with speakers targeting Governor Kotek and the incumbent state senator in the district — four days before the primary election. The city confirmed no new data center applications can clear the approval process before a June 6 moratorium date. A prior petition drive collected 2,400 signatures in six days with no promotion. A June 2 council work session is scheduled. In a community that locked in Google and Amazon property tax breaks through 2051, the reversal is notable.
  • ILNormal’s Six Months, Springfield’s Nine Days: Normal’s moratorium runs through November. Neighboring Bloomington signaled similar support the same week. The statewide POWER Act — which would require developers to fund their own renewable generation, disclose water use, and ban NDAs with local governments — has nine days before the May 31 legislative adjournment. Governor Pritzker has not engaged. Three data center approvals came through since February. The gap between what Normal is doing locally and what Springfield is not doing statewide is now a campaign talking point.
  • TXThe Lone Star Standoff: Hill County’s one-year moratorium is holding despite lawsuit threats. Somervell County passed a formal resolution opposing construction until the legislature acts, possibly 2027. Hays County will revisit its tabled moratorium in June. In Hood County, residents tried and failed twice on a moratorium, then sued MARA Holdings over noise. Bloomberg reported Thursday that a Granbury development commissioner opened a council meeting with a prayer that a rezoning vote would fail. The council voted 4-0 to rezone 2,100 acres for Project Patriot anyway, next to a hospital and school. Attorney General Paxton has been asked by multiple counties for a legal opinion on moratorium authority. No response. The state appears to be running out the clock to the 2027 session while projects get approved.
  • NCRaleigh’s Silence: The Southern Environmental Law Center’s expedited reconsideration petition on NCUC Chair Brawley’s unilateral solar freeze had a response deadline of May 19. As of this morning, no ruling has appeared on the public docket. The April 23 freeze — issued without public hearing, without the other four commissioners, and without an evidentiary record — effectively cancels 770 MW of Duke Energy’s 2026 solar procurement. Every day without a ruling is a day closer to a permanent outcome. If reconsideration is denied, the next move is the courts.
Plays of the Week
  • Portland DSAThe Primary Hammer: Organizing a data center opposition rally four days before the primary election, with a challenger candidate in the crowd naming the incumbent senator, is a specific and replicable play. The Hillsboro rally on May 16 wasn’t primarily about data centers — it was about making incumbents visibly accountable before the vote. The petition baseline (2,400 signatures in six days, no promotion) tells you the latent opposition is there. The rally turned it into an electoral threat. That combination — petition velocity plus candidate-level pressure — is the move for any contested project where a local official has been too accommodating. The lesson for the team’s clients: if you haven’t done the early constituent work before primary season, you’re already behind.
  • Hood County ResidentsThe Noise Vector: Hood County residents filed a personal-injury suit against MARA Holdings, a Bitcoin data center operator, alleging noise-induced headaches, tinnitus, and sleep deprivation. This is a new litigation vector — not an environmental impact challenge or a zoning fight, but a tort claim against an operating facility on behalf of neighbors. MARA says its immersion cooling is quiet. The plaintiffs say otherwise. If this case succeeds, it creates a template that opposition attorneys can deploy against any industrial energy facility with a sustained noise signature — including BESS installations, gas peakers, and large-scale solar inverter stations. Watch the docket. A ruling in favor of the plaintiffs would be cited in every contested permit in every state for years.
  • Cornell / The Nature ConservancyThe 0.17% Defense: A peer-reviewed paper in Geography and Sustainability found that protecting ecologically sensitive lands and prime farmland from utility-scale solar adds a system-wide cost premium of 0.17%. Less than two-tenths of a percent. The framework is built on 46,000 MW of New York State deployment — large enough to be credible at any regulatory body. The play is to run this macro-siting analysis in the pre-permitting phase and bring the map to the planning board before opponents do. “We already avoided the sensitive land” is a more durable position than “we’ll minimize impacts.” For the team’s clients facing farmland opposition, this paper is a tool to hand to the developer at the earliest pre-application meeting.
