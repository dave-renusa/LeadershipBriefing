Happy Monday, all.

Last week the question was who owns the zoning pen. This week the pen is back in local hands, and the calendar is busy. Two municipal data‑center moratoriums come to a head inside twenty‑four hours: Charlotte’s council votes this morning on a 150‑day pause, and Seattle’s full council votes tomorrow on what would be the largest one‑year freeze any U.S. city has adopted. In Pennsylvania a House panel spent Saturday’s news cycle arguing not about whether to slow the buildout but how to power it, with a mandate to put 3,000 megawatts of batteries on the grid. And in Virginia’s Shenandoah Valley, a planning commission handed the county’s first big data‑center campus a 10‑0 denial that now sets up a July fight at the Board of Supervisors. The throughline for the week: the venue is local, the lever is the ordinance, and the calendar is the strategy.

Four standing items, so we don’t re‑litigate them. The New York statewide data‑center moratorium: passed both chambers, delivered to Gov. Hochul on June 4, still unsigned, and she has said she’d rather leave it to municipalities, so a veto stays live. The Illinois POWER Act: no update, summer negotiations continue. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: still no ruling on the reconsideration petition. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand, no timeline set. If any breaks, you’ll get a full item.

The longer look this morning is the Valley, where a county that has never approved a large data center got its first one and treated the application less like a windfall than a warning shot.

Must Read

Clear Brook, Clear No

On Wednesday night, after a hearing that ran close to four hours, the Frederick County Planning Commission voted 10‑0 to recommend denial of Virginia Technology Park, a proposed 220‑acre data‑center campus in Clear Brook, just off Interstate 81 north of Winchester. The applicant, Pennsylvania‑based Equus Capital Partners, wants three parcels rezoned from Rural Areas to Technology Manufacturing for 10 to 11 data‑center buildings, three substation pads, and as many as 288 generators, plus 24,000 square feet of office space. No end‑user has been named. If it is eventually approved, it would be the first large‑scale data center in the county, which is exactly why the room was full.

The engagement story here is a clinic. Before the meeting, about thirty residents marched from the Loudoun Street Mall to the county administration building carrying anti‑data‑center signs and singing “America the Beautiful.” By gavel, the board room had exceeded capacity and the fire marshal was called; the overflow watched on a television in the break room. Roughly fifty people spoke, taking more than two hours. The opposition did not lead with slogans. It led with the proffers: a resident on a private well warned about cooling‑system discharge into the Opequon Creek watershed; an independent acoustics consultant testified the campus would be “six to eight times louder” than current ambient noise, and a physician pressed on low‑frequency sound the applicant’s own study admitted it could not measure below the 63‑hertz band. Commissioner Elizabeth Kozel summed up the room: “There’s not enough meat, there’s not enough detail, and there’s not enough enforceable items within the proffers that we can dig our teeth into.”

The recommendation is only that. The proposal goes to the Frederick County Board of Supervisors on July 8 to be approved, denied, or tabled, and that is where the real decision lives. Two wrinkles make the next month worth watching. First, the proffer fight is the whole game: the applicant says it will cap water at 120,000 gallons a day and stay under the county’s 60‑dBA residential noise limit, while residents argue a fine is no deterrent to a billion‑dollar tenant and that the proffers don’t even define when the 288 generators may run. Second, the politics are now electoral: a sitting planning commissioner and a county resident announced at the march that they are gathering signatures to run as independents in November’s special elections for a supervisor seat and the board chairmanship, with a June 16 filing deadline. The data center is becoming the ballot question.

The Takeaway

A unanimous “no” from a planning commission is a recommendation, not a result, and the gap between Wednesday’s vote and the July 8 supervisors’ meeting is precisely the window where engagement is won or lost. Two lessons for our work. First, the opposition that wins on proffers is the opposition that read them: noise thresholds, generator run‑time definitions, water‑discharge points, and enforceability are where a campaign turns “we’re worried” into “the application is incomplete.” That is the language commissioners can vote on. Second, when a contested project becomes a campaign issue with a filing deadline attached, the decision timeline and the election timeline merge, and any client carrying an application into a Virginia fall election needs an engagement plan that accounts for candidates running expressly against the project.

Also On Our Radar
  • Keystone Wants More in the Jar: Pennsylvania’s House Energy committee took testimony Saturday on House Bill 2380, which would order the state’s largest utilities to install 3,000 megawatts of battery storage by 2033, split into 2,000 megawatts of short‑duration and 1,000 megawatts of long‑duration capacity and parceled out by the PUC across companies with more than 600,000 customers. The framing is what makes it our story: with PJM auction prices driving a second straight summer of rate hikes, storage is being pitched as a “non‑wire” fix that is cheaper to justify than new transmission, and the PUC chair called batteries a proven grid asset, while warning the 3,000‑megawatt target may be “overly aggressive and potentially unachievable.” Pennsylvania ranks 28th in the country in storage today. For BESS developers, the signal is that the state‑mandate path and the local‑siting fight are about to run on the same track, and the siting fine print our clients negotiate at the township level is the bottleneck that any megawatt mandate eventually slams into.
  • Emerald City, Hard Stop: Seattle’s City Council votes tomorrow on a one‑year moratorium covering data centers drawing 10 megawatts or more, after two committees advanced it. If it passes, Seattle becomes the largest U.S. jurisdiction to freeze the sector, and it would use the year to study power, water, and neighborhood impacts. Watch the threshold: a 10‑megawatt trigger is far lower than New York’s 20‑megawatt statewide line, which means a big‑city pause can sweep in mid‑sized projects that a state bill would wave through. Washington is in our footprint, and the venue‑by‑venue threshold map is becoming the single most important number for siting strategy.
Project Updates
  • NCCharlotte’s City Council is set to vote this morning on a 150‑day moratorium on new data centers, a pause that would run into October while the city writes rules. The pressure point is structural: data centers can currently be built “by right” in eight Charlotte zoning districts, including next to homes and in Uptown, with no council approval, and the council inched toward the vote after a May 26 public meeting. Opponents are pointing at the proposed Powerhouse Charlotte campus in the north of the city, a five‑building project on 122 acres that could draw 300 to 400 megawatts. North Carolina is in our footprint; today’s vote is the one to watch on the docket.

On the Frontlines
  • PAUpper Burrell Won’t Take the State’s Word: Dozens of Upper Burrell Township residents packed a Penn State New Kensington classroom Thursday to learn how to fight a planned hyperscale data center at the former Alcoa research campus, a 1,400‑acre site where developer TECfusions has not yet made its plans public. The township already passed a 180‑day moratorium last month to buy time to write local law, and the organizing group Protect PT used the meeting to walk neighbors through the zoning process and the noise and gas‑plant concerns. The tell is the mood toward Harrisburg: the Shapiro administration’s new voluntary data‑center standards (a third of new load from “clean, firm” sources by 2035, developers covering their own grid upgrades) drew the line of the week from Protect PT’s director: “Policies that have no teeth don’t mean anything if you don’t have strong laws in place.” Residents who lived through the fracking debate are not interested in voluntary, and they’ve learned that local ordinances can be stricter than the state floor. With a second hyperscale campus already approved nearby in Springdale, the Allegheny Valley is teaching the exact lesson our work turns on: the binding rules get written at the township, not the capitol.
