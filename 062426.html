Good morning, all.

For a year we have charted the data-center fight as a land-use story — a contest over cornfields, viewsheds, water tables, and the zoning vocabulary a county did or didn’t have. The deep dive below argues the center of gravity has moved. The most powerful argument against a data center is no longer the noise of the cooling fans or the conversion of farmland; it is the line item on a voter’s electric bill. That argument is bipartisan, it is portable to every state inside an RTO, and this week a national poll put a number on it that should change how every developer — ours included — walks into a hearing room.

The reason it matters now is timing. The capacity costs from PJM’s record-setting auctions start flowing onto bills across thirteen states this month, just as a fresh survey finds a clear majority of voters — Republicans included — ready to pause new data centers outright. When the grievance stops being “not in my backyard” and becomes “not on my bill,” opposition stops being a local nuisance and becomes a kitchen-table issue with a dollar sign. That is a harder thing to engage, and a more important one to get right.

Five standing items, so we don’t re-litigate them. New York’s statewide data-center moratorium: still unsigned on Gov. Hochul’s desk — and, as the deep dive notes, now carrying fresh polling pressure. The Illinois POWER Act: no update. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: still no ruling on the reconsideration petition. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand. And Sangamon County’s buffer-rezoning gambit: still no dated outcome posted from the zoning committee, so we continue to hold it. If any breaks, you’ll get a full item.

Must Read

The Bill Comes Due

The data-center fight has found the one argument that travels everywhere, crosses party lines, and doesn’t depend on whether a project is in your county at all: your electric bill. This week the politics caught up to the math. A new Data for Progress survey, fielded June 12–16 and released Monday, found 63% of likely voters back a pause on new AI data-center construction — and the striking part isn’t the topline, it’s the spread: 67% of Democrats, 66% of independents, and 58% of Republicans. A construction issue that breaks 58% on the right is not a partisan issue. It is a consensus, and consensus is what moratoriums are made of.

What turned the corner is cost. For most of this year the opposition’s case rested on water draw, land conversion, noise, and the conversion of rural acreage to industrial use without permanent jobs — real grievances, but local ones, easy for a developer to answer parcel by parcel. The bill argument is different because it lands on people who will never see the project. PJM’s market monitor found that data centers accounted for $6.5 billion — about 40% — of the $16.4 billion in capacity costs cleared in the grid operator’s last auction, most of it for facilities not yet built. IEEFA, tracing the same trend, found data-center load has driven PJM capacity prices up by a factor of ten — and those charges start flowing onto ratepayer bills across PJM’s thirteen-state footprint beginning this month. The next auction, for the 2028/29 delivery year, clears in June with no price collar to cap the next increase. The voter feeling the squeeze in Ohio or New Jersey doesn’t need a data center next door to be angry about one.

That reframing is exactly what we flagged last week when a single tally put roughly $130 billion in data-center projects blocked or delayed in the first quarter alone. The community-risk line that capital used to wave off as a vibe now has a quarter-over-quarter number behind it — and the new poll supplies the political half of the same equation. Put them together and the message to a county board is unambiguous: saying no to a data center is no longer a fringe position a supervisor has to spend capital on. It is the safe vote, the one that polls 60%, the one a primary opponent will use against you if you don’t take it. When the no vote becomes the politically cautious vote, the entire burden of proof flips onto the developer.

The Takeaway

The engagement playbook has to answer the bill, not just the viewshed. Host-community benefits, job numbers, and water-mitigation plans still matter — but they no longer address the grievance that actually moves a statewide poll, because that grievance belongs to ratepayers who get none of the benefits and all of the bill. The developers who win the room over the next eighteen months will be the ones who walk in with a cost-isolation story already written: a binding commitment that the project pays for its own generation, transmission, and capacity, and that the load does not socialize its costs onto the neighbors’ meters. Regulators from Georgia to Ohio are already writing that principle into tariff rules; the smart developer brings it to the hearing voluntarily, before a hostile board or a 63%-backed legislature writes a harsher version for them. The fight used to be about the land. It is now about who pays for the power — and that is a question our renewable clients should expect to be asked too, the moment a wind or solar project needs new wires to reach the grid.

Also On Our Radar
  • The Permian Pause: the Public Utility Commission of Texas abated — effectively paused — one of five proposed 765-kV transmission segments in the Permian Basin Reliability Plan after an organized landowner revolt, sending commissioners back to revisit threshold questions before any routing decision. The throughline to our work is the notice fight underneath it: 179 landowners moved to halt the case on the grounds that route changes violated state notice requirements, and administrative law judges denied them more time on June 1, citing statutory deadlines. The whole Permian buildout is estimated near $33 billion to construct and approaching $100 billion over its life, and the opposition has already learned the lesson Virginia taught the data-center world — that the cleanest place to stop a project is the paperwork, not the merits. Transmission is the connective tissue every renewable build depends on, and it is now drawing the same packed-hearing, eminent-domain, defective-notice fight that data centers do. For any client whose project needs new high-voltage wire to reach load, the Permian is the warning: the line is as contestable as the panels.
  • Arizona’s Split Screen: two Arizona developments this month show how unevenly the renewable build is landing. On the supply side, it’s moving — EDP Renewables and Salt River Project commissioned the 200-MW / 800-MWh Flatland battery system in Pinal County on June 11, an evening-capacity anchor for a solar-heavy grid and a reminder that storage still gets built where a utility wants the megawatts and a host community sees the tax base. On the customer side, the rules just got friendlier too: the Arizona Court of Appeals on June 16 overturned an extra grid-access fee APS had been charging rooftop-solar customers, finding the utility imposed it without adequate due process. It’s a small ruling with a portable principle — the same one running under this issue’s lead: when a utility wants to assign costs to a class of customers, the process by which it does so is now where the fight is won or lost. Clean receipts beat a clean argument, on the solar side as much as the data-center side.
On the Frontlines
  • NYStill on the Desk: the first-in-the-nation statewide data-center moratorium remains unsigned on Gov. Hochul’s desk, the one-year pause on DEC permits for facilities of 20 MW or larger now sitting in the exact spot where this issue’s polling lands hardest. Hochul has voiced a preference for letting municipalities decide and is up for re-election; the 63% statewide number gives the bill’s backers a fresh cudgel and gives her a harder politics to thread. No signature, no veto — but the clock and the polls are both running.
  • INNew Albany’s First Read: New Albany advanced a proposed one-year data-center moratorium to a first reading this week, with a public meeting set for June 22, after Mayor Jeff Gahan announced the push on June 9. It’s the now-familiar shape — a city reaching for a pause to buy time to write the definitions and standards its ordinance never anticipated — and another Indiana jurisdiction joining a list that already runs through Fort Wayne and a string of county moratoriums.
  • NVReno Taps the Brakes: Reno paused new data-center approvals while it sorts out how to weigh the water and power demands against the tax revenue, putting the desert Southwest’s fastest-growing market into the same study-first posture spreading across the country. The open question the city now has to answer is the one our lead is about: who pays for the load it does let in.
  • CALithium Valley Locks Up: Imperial County’s supervisors unanimously approved a 45-day data-center moratorium on June 16 — extendable past ten months — over objections that it created loopholes rather than guardrails. The wrinkle for our storage clients stands: the ordinance defines “data center” broadly enough to sweep in battery systems, substations, and gen-tie lines, so a pause aimed at server farms can catch a BESS project in the same net.
  • WABattery Backlash, Wider Still: the standalone-storage opposition we flagged Friday drew statewide and regional coverage this week as resistance spread across western Washington, with Snoqualmie, Carnation, North Bend, Puyallup, Duvall, and Kitsap County all moving on moratoriums in the past month over fire and toxic-smoke fears. The safety narrative that stalled Moss Landing has fully colonized the Northwest, and it is hitting the storage half of the build hardest — the single clearest sign that “renewable” is no longer a shield.
  • MIWires, Public Comment, Pushback: ITC opened a public-input window on a Michigan transmission project even as landowner opposition persists, the Midwest echo of the Permian and Maryland fights — an interstate grid build colliding with the people whose land the towers cross. The engagement lesson repeats: the route-selection meeting, not the permit hearing, is now where transmission projects are won or lost.
  • TXThe Build Still Closes: amid the noise, capital still flows — OCI Energy and Arava Power closed roughly $394 million in construction financing for the 347-MW SunRoper solar project in Wharton County, backed by a 20-year PPA with a Fortune 100 offtaker and targeting commercial operation in 2027. It’s the counter-fact to keep in view: the solar pipeline is still pricing and closing, with the EIA projecting a record 86 GW of new U.S. capacity this year. The fights are loud, but the build hasn’t stopped — it’s just gotten harder to site.
