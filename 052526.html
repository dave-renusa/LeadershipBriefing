The pattern is locked in: state legislatures run out the clock while city halls rewrite the rules. Minneapolis voted 8–5 Thursday to impose a six-month moratorium on new data centers, exempting only small downtown proposals. East Vincent Township, Pennsylvania voted unanimously Friday to reject a 1.9 million square foot data center proposal on the former Pennhurst property. In New York, nearly 500 small businesses sent a letter to Governor Hochul demanding the state’s three-year moratorium bill move before session ends. Every one of those actions happened between Tuesday and Friday of last week. The pace is not slowing.

Meanwhile, the legislative windows are closing fast. Illinois has six days before the May 31 adjournment. The POWER Act — which would require data centers to fund their own renewables, disclose water use, and ban NDAs with local governments — still has not moved beyond committee. Advocates publicly accused Governor Pritzker of “lack of engagement” May 14. The May 31 deadline is the last real shot before veto session in October. In North Carolina, the NCUC’s remaining four commissioners still have not acted on the May 19 reconsideration deadline for Chair Brawley’s unilateral solar freeze. No ruling has appeared on the public docket. Every day without one is a day closer to 770 MW of solar procurement going dark for 2026.

In Texas, the federal wind permit freeze is deepening. The Defense Department has not approved a single wind project since August 2025 and canceled all pending developer meetings in April. Fifty-four Texas projects are stuck, part of a 165-project national logjam. Add it to the stack alongside the data center county fights, the NC solar freeze, and the Illinois legislative crunch, and this is a week where clients need eyes on multiple dockets simultaneously. Use the long weekend to catch up. The work resumes tomorrow.

Must Read

Springfield’s Last Six Days

The Illinois POWER Act has one window left before the state legislature adjourns May 31. Senate Bill 4016 would require hyperscale data centers to fund their own renewable generation, obtain water-use permits from the Illinois EPA, disclose energy and water consumption metrics, assess noise and pollution impacts on surrounding communities, and — the provision that matters most to community-engagement practitioners — ban non-disclosure agreements between developers and local governments. That last clause would prohibit the quiet benefit-package negotiations that have historically neutralized local opposition before it can organize.

The bill has had subject-matter hearings and gone no further. Advocates publicly accused Governor Pritzker of “lack of engagement” as recently as May 14 — a notably sharp posture for advocacy groups that need that same governor to sign any bill that passes. The political dynamic is familiar: state-level action requires a legislative coalition that takes months to assemble, and that coalition is not assembled. Normal, Illinois imposed a preemptive six-month moratorium in mid-May without a single data center application on its docket. Springfield has had the POWER Act in committee since February.

If the bill dies May 31, advocates have signaled they will target the fall veto session — October at the earliest. That means the next real legislative window opens four months from now, with three more data center approvals already processed in Illinois since February. The gap between what municipalities are doing locally and what the state is not doing has become a campaign argument in competitive districts. Three approvals in five months while the regulatory bill sits in committee is the kind of record that challengers run on.

For the team: any client with a contested data center project in Illinois is operating in a state where the political environment is shifting fast but the legal framework has not caught up. The NDA ban in the POWER Act is especially relevant to engagement strategy. If it passes, the closed-door benefit-package negotiations that defuse early opposition become illegal. Get ahead of that conversation with clients now, before the bill’s status is settled.

⚡ The Takeaway: Illinois has six days to pass the most consequential state-level data center regulation proposed anywhere in the country. If the POWER Act dies Saturday, the municipal moratorium model — Normal, Bloomington, and whichever township files next — becomes the only active regulatory mechanism in the state until October. Either way, the engagement environment for Illinois data center projects is changing faster than any permitting calendar accounts for.
Also On Our Radar
  • The Minneapolis Line: Minneapolis City Council voted 8–5 Thursday to impose a six-month moratorium on new data centers, exempting downtown proposals under 350,000 square feet in a compromise offered by Council Vice President Jamal Osman. The original bill was a full year. Even the narrower version required eight council members to publicly back a moratorium in a city that had received no significant opposition petition — reinforcing the preemptive posture Normal, Illinois demonstrated two weeks ago. The political threshold for a moratorium vote is now lower than the threshold for a single constituent complaint at a planning board meeting. That is a structural shift, not an isolated vote.
  • The Small Business Bloc: Nearly 500 New York small businesses signed a letter to Governor Hochul and the legislature May 21 calling for the state’s three-year moratorium bill on hyperscale data center construction. The signatories are Finger Lakes wineries, agricultural operations, and tourism businesses — exactly the economic constituency that county-level data center opposition has been trying to organize for two years. This is not a standard environmental-NGO letter. It is an economic-constituency play that reframes the debate from “NIMBYism” to “business community opposition,” which is considerably harder for a governor to dismiss as irrational. A Capitol rally drew 100+ supporters two weeks earlier. Hochul has not committed.
  • The Pentagon’s Thumb on the Scale: The Defense Department has not approved a single wind project since August 2025 and canceled all pending developer meetings in April, leaving 54 Texas wind projects and 165 nationwide in limbo, according to the Texas Tribune. Federal law requires DoD review within 60 days of FAA referral. That deadline is being routinely missed with no public explanation. The agency is not rejecting projects outright — it is simply not acting. Developers cannot secure financing, local permits contingent on federal clearance are at risk, and construction timelines are slipping. The administration cannot flat-out stop land-based wind federally, so bureaucratic delay is the instrument. For any client with a Texas wind project in the permitting queue, this is a policy decision expressed through inaction, not a temporary bottleneck.
On the Frontlines
  • PAChester County’s Unanimous No: East Vincent Township supervisors voted unanimously Friday to deny Pennhurst Holdings LLC a conditional use permit for a 1.9 million square foot data center on the former Pennhurst State School property near the Schuylkill River — adjacent to homes and the Southeastern Veterans Center. The February planning commission recommendation against the project held. State Senator Katie Muth announced plans to file a three-year state moratorium bill. A developer also withdrew an expanded data center application in nearby East Whiteland the same week. Eastern Pennsylvania is no longer a soft landing zone for speculative data center proposals.
  • ILSix Days and Counting: The Illinois General Assembly adjourns May 31. The POWER Act remains in committee with no floor vote scheduled. Advocates publicly accused Governor Pritzker of “lack of engagement” May 14. Normal’s six-month moratorium runs through November; neighboring Bloomington has signaled support for a matching measure. Three data center approvals have processed in Illinois since February. If the bill dies Saturday, the fall veto session in October is the next legislative opening.
  • VAThe Writ Panel Week: A three-justice panel of the Virginia Supreme Court is scheduled for a 20-minute argument session in late May or early June on QTS’s appeal of the Digital Gateway data center project — 37 data centers, 22 million square feet, directly adjacent to the Manassas battlefield. The court’s decision whether to accept the appeal is entirely discretionary. QTS filed on April 30, hours before the deadline. Prince William County and co-developer Compass Datacenters have already withdrawn. If the court declines, the Court of Appeals ruling stands and the project is dead. If the court takes it, the fight runs at least another year while the clock runs on the project’s economics.
  • NCThe Silence Speaks: The reconsideration petition on Chair Brawley’s unilateral solar freeze had a response deadline of May 19. No ruling has appeared on the public docket as of this morning. The April 23 freeze — issued by Brawley alone, without the other four commissioners, without a public hearing, without an evidentiary record — effectively cancels Duke Energy’s entire 2026 solar procurement cycle. Governor Stein’s office called it a decision that “will only make things more expensive.” Every day the silence holds is a day closer to a permanent outcome. If reconsideration is denied, the courts are next.
  • TXThe Invisible Freeze: Fifty-four Texas wind projects are waiting for DoD permits that have not been issued since August 2025. The department canceled all developer meetings in April. Federal law requires 60-day review. There is no public explanation for why deadlines are being missed. The delay is disrupting financing timelines, jeopardizing local permits contingent on federal clearance, and pushing construction schedules. Texas has more wind turbines than any other state and 17 military bases with flight facilities — the review framework was designed to balance both interests. Right now it is serving only one of them.
  • NYHudson Valley Draws a Line: Hudson Valley residents rallied Friday against a massive proposed data center in East Fishkill, the same week nearly 500 New York small businesses sent a joint letter to Governor Hochul backing the state moratorium bill. The letter’s lead voices are Finger Lakes wineries and farm operations — businesses that frame the fight in economic, not environmental, terms. Governor Hochul has not committed to signing the bill. The gap between the energy of the constituency and the silence from the executive is the story to watch as the legislative session nears its end.
