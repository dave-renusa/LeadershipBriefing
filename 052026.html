Volume 5 · Wednesday, May 20, 2026

Good morning.

The opposition stopped being local this week. What started as a patchwork of county board fights, NIMBY petitions, and six-month moratoriums has graduated into something else: a coordinated, multi-state, multi-tactic campaign against data center development that is now landing in governors' offices and state capitols simultaneously. In the past seven days, more than 60 environmental and labor groups petitioned New Jersey's governor for an emergency moratorium, New York lawmakers rallied at the Capitol for a three-year statewide ban, Pennsylvania residents staged a 225-person online town hall — and the national count of active moratorium and ban efforts hit 78, up from 8 a year ago.

The pattern matters more than any single fight. Opposition groups have figured out that local wins don't stop the industry — developers just move to the next county. The new play is statewide pressure at the legislative and executive level, simultaneously, across multiple states. New Jersey's coalition is using utility-bill affordability as the frame. New York's is using environmental justice. Pennsylvania's is using transparency and zoning rights. Illinois is using a 31-day clock. Different frames, same pressure point, same week.

Virginia's Governor Spanberger signed legislation last Friday handing the State Corporation Commission broad new authority to make data centers pay their fair share of grid costs — with a critical catch that puts everything back in regulators' hands. And in North Carolina, yesterday's deadline for the NCUC to rule on the solar freeze reconsideration petition has passed. We're watching the docket.

Must Read

78 and Counting

A year ago, eight local and state government efforts to enact moratoriums or bans on data center development were active across the United States. Today there are 78, according to Deseret News reporting published Saturday, citing the U.S. Data Center Moratorium Tracker. That is not a trend line. That is a movement arriving at scale — and this week it arrived in three states at once.

The most significant development is that the opposition has stopped fighting at the county level and started fighting at the state level simultaneously. In New Jersey, more than 60 environmental, labor, and community groups sent a letter to Governor Mikie Sherrill on Monday demanding an emergency moratorium on any data center drawing 20 megawatts or more. The letter, organized by Food & Water Watch and the Pinelands Preservation Alliance, calls data center expansion "one of the biggest environmental and social threats of our generation" and specifically ties it to rising utility bills — a politically potent frame for a governor who declared a state of emergency over electricity costs on inauguration day and hasn't solved it. The coalition has 7,500 petition signatures and a Fairleigh Dickinson poll found 65 percent of registered New Jersey voters support a ban. That's a very large number for a policy position most voters hadn't heard of two years ago. The governor's office has not responded.

In New York, nearly 100 residents, lawmakers, and advocates rallied at the state Capitol on May 13 for S.9144/A.10141 — a bill that would impose a three-year moratorium statewide while the Department of Environmental Conservation conducts impact reviews with nine regional public hearings. State Sen. Kristin Gonzalez, chair of the Senate's Internet and Technology Committee and the bill's co-sponsor, framed it as a power struggle with big tech: "They have been defining and setting the rules for how they operate and how they profit and we have been living with the consequences." Governor Hochul has signaled a preference for regulatory reform over a blanket pause, which means the bill's path depends on whether the coalition can generate the constituent pressure that moves legislators past a governor's preference. Food & Water Watch organized 50 post-rally lobby meetings with legislative offices the same afternoon. That's the mechanics of the campaign, not the rally — the rally was the announcement.

In Illinois, the POWER Act now has 11 days before the General Assembly adjourns May 31, and Governor Pritzker still hasn't engaged. The bill would require community benefits agreements, ban NDAs between developers and local governments, mandate water-use disclosures, and force developers to build their own renewable power. It passed subject-matter hearings in February and hasn't moved since. Three data center projects were approved in Illinois after the bill was introduced. ComEd's territory has nearly 100 large-load projects queued for interconnection. Advocates from the Illinois Clean Jobs Coalition are publicly accusing Pritzker of "lack of engagement," and bipartisan appetite for some version of regulation exists — Republicans like Rep. Jed Davis say their constituents feel silenced. The window closes in eleven days. Silence from Springfield is a choice with consequences.

⚡ The Takeaway: The data center opposition has learned what the renewable industry already knows: local wins don't hold when developers can shop jurisdictions. The move to simultaneous statewide moratorium campaigns — multi-coalition, issue-specific, landing in multiple states at once — is the opposition's answer to that lesson. Utility affordability (NJ), environmental justice (NY), transparency (PA), a legislative clock (IL): different frames, same pressure point. For the team, this is a fundamentally different engagement environment than twelve months ago. Communities don't just oppose projects anymore. They organize coalitions, go to the statehouse, and come with polling data.
Also On Our Radar
  • Dominion's Deal, SCC's Call: Virginia Governor Abigail Spanberger signed legislation last Friday directing the State Corporation Commission to take "all steps necessary" to ensure residential customers do not pay generation and distribution costs meant to serve data centers. The law also extends Dominion Energy's authority to spend up to $900,000 per mile burying local distribution lines while recovering costs and maintaining its profit margin — a program that has added $4.88 per month to the typical residential customer's bill. Spanberger amended out the most prescriptive data center cost-allocation language during the session and replaced it with broad SCC discretion, then secured a private "commitment" from Dominion to limit undergrounding to areas most exposed to extreme weather. PJM capacity market costs ran $28/MWh in 2023 and $329/MWh in 2025. Those numbers live in every Virginia ratepayer's bill. What the SCC does next determines whether Virginia's affordability politics and its data-center-heavy grid can actually be reconciled.
  • The Recall Escalator: Last Thursday in Calipatria, California, residents served recall notices on the mayor, mayor pro tem, and a council member — then watched the same council vote 4-0 to initiate moratorium hearings for data centers, potentially making Calipatria the first jurisdiction in Imperial County to enact one. The opening and closing moves happened on the same dais, on the same night. This is a pattern emerging at the county level: opposition coalitions skip the election cycle and use recall mechanisms to create immediate political risk for officials seen as too accommodating. The simultaneity — recall filed, moratorium voted, one meeting — is a new tactic. It tells every elected official in the region that a favorable data center vote has a political half-life measured in months, not years.
  • Pennsylvania's Number: More than 225 people joined an online town hall May 14 organized by the Better Path Coalition, with speakers targeting Governor Shapiro — who wasn't present and hadn't responded to invitations — for what they described as a transparency failure. Pennsylvania has nearly 60 data centers proposed, approved, or under construction. A February Quinnipiac poll found 68 percent of registered voters would oppose an AI data center in their community. The "Pennsylvania Data Center Resistance" Facebook group, started in January, now has more than 12,000 members. State Sen. Katie Muth plans to introduce a three-year moratorium bill. Republican Rep. Jamie Walsh told attendees that Pennsylvania became a target because it doesn't tax data center equipment the way Virginia does — and that fact is now in the opposition's standard presentation. When bipartisan frustration and a 12,000-member Facebook group find a legislative vehicle, the timeline compresses.
On the Frontlines
  • NJThe Governor's Inbox: More than 60 organizations formally petitioned Governor Sherrill this week for an emergency moratorium on data centers drawing 20 MW or more, framing the request around utility affordability and PFAS concerns from cooling systems. The Philadelphia Inquirer confirmed Monday the governor's office has not responded. The political pinch is real: Sherrill declared a utility emergency on inauguration day, and the data center industry is the most visible driver of the rate increases she promised to fix. The Pinelands region already has multiple municipalities passing local ordinances. The statewide moratorium petition is the escalation.
  • NYAlbany's Three-Year Ask: Lawmakers and advocates rallied at the Capitol on May 13 for a bill that would pause any new data center capable of drawing 20 MW or more for three years while a Generic Environmental Impact Statement with nine regional public hearings is completed. Alabama, New York — facing a proposed $19.4 billion, 2.2-million-square-foot facility — was prominently in the crowd. Governor Hochul prefers regulatory action over a blanket ban, setting up a collision between the executive and a growing legislative coalition backed by polling, a petition, and 50 legislative office visits organized on the same day as the rally.
  • PAGround Zero Tax: The May 14 town hall featured Republican Rep. Jamie Walsh telling 225 attendees that Pennsylvania became the data center industry's target because it doesn't collect a sales and use tax on data center equipment — unlike Virginia. That argument, landing in a cross-partisan context, could be the wedge that moves something in Harrisburg. Sen. Muth's forthcoming moratorium bill, combined with municipal-level zoning fights from Archbald to East Whiteland to Conshohocken, is making Pennsylvania contested in ways it wasn't a year ago.
  • VASCC's Discretion: Governor Spanberger signed the Dominion-backed bills last Friday. Inside Climate News reported the most prescriptive data center cost-allocation language was stripped out during session and replaced with SCC authority to act "in its sole discretion." That means Virginia's promise to make data centers pay their fair share now lives or dies at the commission level, not in statute. With PJM capacity costs having risen more than tenfold since 2023, the SCC's first major ruling under this new authority will be watched by regulators and advocates in every PJM state.
  • ILEleven Days and Counting: The POWER Act requires floor action before May 31 adjournment. As of last Thursday, the bill remains in subject-matter committee limbo, with Pritzker's office monitoring "fiscal impact" and offering nothing resembling a floor commitment. Three data center approvals have come in since February. One hundred large-load interconnection projects sit queued in the ComEd territory. If the session closes without a bill, every one of those fights reverts to county boards — and the opposition networks built over the past year start over at square one, jurisdiction by jurisdiction.
  • NCWaiting on Raleigh: The Southern Environmental Law Center's petition asking the NC Utilities Commission to grant expedited reconsideration of Chair Brawley's unilateral April 23 solar freeze order came due yesterday, May 19. As of this morning, no ruling has been confirmed on the docket. The order — issued by the chair alone, without the other four commissioners, without public notice — froze Duke Energy's entire 2026 solar procurement cycle and effectively canceled the 770 MW of planned new generation. If the commission denies reconsideration, the next move is the courts. Duke's pending rate case before the same body runs concurrently. The two proceedings create leverage that both sides will use.
