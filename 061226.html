Friday morning, team.

The wrap on this week is that the fight no longer respects the sector. We watched a 16‑megawatt solar farm die in south Georgia, a battery campus get a 7‑1 thumbs‑down from a Virginia planning commission, a battery moratorium hit a Long Island town board, and a 1,150‑megawatt wind‑and‑solar complex in Washington land in front of the state Supreme Court — all inside seven days, and all carrying the same grammar of organized neighbors, fire‑and‑water arguments, and ordinances written to say no. Data centers are still drawing the biggest crowds, but the opposition playbook our clients meet on a solar or storage site is now identical to the one a hyperscaler meets, down to the Moss Landing references and the petition counts. The sectors have converged on a single problem: permission.

The counter‑moves are getting sharper too, and they are the part worth studying. In Clinton, Iowa, a city council looked at fifty‑two angry speakers and still voted the moratorium down, which is a reminder that a packed room is not the same as a majority on the dais. In Bowie County, Texas, a developer announced a solar deal by naming the school district, the college, and the fire district that get the tax check — benefits framing with an address on it. And in Marion County, Kansas, a county that already lives next to a wind farm showed everyone how to put teeth in a contract after the turbines are up. The two Plays at the bottom are built around those last two.

Four standing items, so we don’t re‑litigate them. The New York statewide data‑center moratorium: no update, still unsigned on Gov. Hochul’s desk. The Illinois POWER Act: no update. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: still no ruling on the reconsideration petition. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand. If any breaks, you’ll get a full item.

Also On Our Radar
  • Horse Heaven Heads to the High Court: Scout Clean Energy’s Horse Heaven project — roughly 72,500 acres of turbines, three solar arrays, and up to 1,150 megawatts in the Tri‑Cities — reached the Washington Supreme Court, with oral argument set for Thursday. Tri‑Cities CARES, Benton County, and the Yakama Nation argue the state’s Energy Facility Site Evaluation Council and former Gov. Inslee cut procedural corners, restoring turbine placements over wildlife, tribal, and viewshed objections. This is the endgame of the state‑siting path we keep flagging: when the regulator approves over local and tribal objection, the opposition does not fold, it appeals, and the project spends another year in a courtroom instead of construction. Washington is in our footprint, and any client riding a state‑council approval should read this as the bill that comes due after the permit.
  • Peach State Pushback: The Lee County Board of Commissioners voted 4‑1 Tuesday night to deny Inman Solar a permit for a 15.75‑megawatt array on roughly 69 acres off Fussell Road, after staff and the planning commission had both recommended denial. The losing argument was not aesthetics — it was hydrology: neighbors pointed at drainage complaints already dogging a nearby Silicon Ranch site and warned that a hundred acres of panels behaves “like a large impervious surface,” with the runoff draining toward a sensitive creek and sinkhole system. Inman had stacked the table with proffers — pre‑ and post‑construction well testing, $200,000 toward road improvements, a $30,000 maintenance fund, an estimated $700,000 in tax revenue over five years — and still lost, because the stormwater case was specific and the benefits case was generic. A clinic in why the technical objection beats the goodwill package.
  • Music City Says Not So Fast: Nashville’s Metro Council passed the first of three readings, 26‑1, on a temporary moratorium on new data centers, after backlash over proposals sited next to the Nashville Zoo and at Fisk University drew a petition north of 360,000 signatures. First reading is the easy one and two more votes remain, but a 26‑1 margin and a six‑figure petition mean the political price of a yes vote has already been set. Tennessee is in our footprint; the tell here is the targeting — opposition that anchors a fight to a beloved institution (a zoo, a historically Black university) converts a zoning question into a values question, and values questions are the ones that fill council chambers.
Project Updates
  • IAClinton’s City Council voted down a 120‑day data‑center moratorium 5‑2 even after fifty‑two residents — nearly all of them in favor of the pause — packed the chamber over a proposed $10 billion QTS facility. The councilwoman who sponsored the measure left the dais in protest after the vote. The counter‑lesson to the rest of this week: a full room and a hostile mic line do not guarantee the count, and a developer with the council majority can absorb a loud hearing and still win the night.
  • KSLawrence’s City Commission, facing a standing‑room crowd waving anti‑data‑center signs, directed staff to bring back a moratorium proposal within thirty days, with several residents pressing for a pause as long as three years. No ordinance yet — this is the step before the step — but the direction is set and the thirty‑day clock is the one to watch.
  • KSReno County’s commission, in a split vote, rejected proposed bans on both data centers and battery storage — an outlier in a week full of pauses — even as individual commissioners voiced opposition to a specific battery site under discussion. The split is the story: the same board can decline a blanket ban and still telegraph that a particular project is in trouble, which is exactly the gap a site‑specific engagement plan has to fill.
  • NYHuntington’s Town Board put a six‑month, townwide moratorium on battery‑storage permits and approvals on its June 9 agenda for adoption (Local Law 17‑2026). Long Island remains the densest cluster of BESS moratoriums in the country, and Huntington adds the largest town on the North Shore to the list. For storage developers, the Island is now a map of closed doors with staggered expiration dates — and the entitlement question is less “will they say no” than “when does the window reopen.”
  • VADenver‑based Tract filed a conditional‑use application for “Tuckahoe Technology Park,” a 900‑acre, 12‑building data‑center campus in Goochland’s contested Technology Overlay District — the first project to test the overlay, and one arriving with a lawsuit over the district’s adoption still pending and just days after neighboring Hanover rejected Tract’s Mountain Road project. Goochland has been the county that says yes while its neighbors say no; this application is where that reputation gets stress‑tested in front of organized opposition.

On the Frontlines
  • MISay No to BESS: More than a hundred neighbors rallied at Little Pistol Farms in Oshtemo Township Monday night against a battery‑storage site that out‑of‑state developer NewEdge Renewable Power has signaled it will file on along Van Kal Avenue in the second half of the year. The organizing is the story: the “Say No to BESS” group is openly recruiting local attorneys, realtors, engineers, and scientists to help the township write its ordinance — the checklist a developer will later have to satisfy — before any application arrives, while the township’s one‑year moratorium runs. They understand the leverage exactly right. Under Michigan’s Public Act 233, the Public Service Commission can override a local denial, but the developer still has to meet local standards, so the residents writing those standards now are setting the terms of a fight that hasn’t formally started. Next planning‑commission meeting is June 25.
  • VAChesapeake Says Cool It: Chesapeake’s Planning Commission voted 7‑1 to recommend the City Council deny East Point Energy a conditional‑use permit for the Mill Stone battery‑storage project after roughly three hours of public comment dominated by fire‑risk fears, with residents invoking the Moss Landing fire by name. The recommendation is not the final word — the council takes it up next month — but the dynamic should look familiar from the Arizona battery fight we tracked Wednesday: the commission lays down a denial, the developer gets one more month to change the room, and the council vote becomes the real contest. The throughline for any storage client in Virginia is that “Moss Landing” is now a one‑word argument, and the burden is on the applicant to make fire suppression and setbacks concrete before the first hearing, not after.
Plays of the Week
  • RWEPut an Address on the Benefits: announcing a long‑term power deal with Meta for the 298‑megawatt Rabbit’s Foot Solar project in Bowie County, Texas, RWE did not lead with “jobs and tax base.” It led with roughly $50 million in tax revenue over forty years, itemized to the institutions residents actually care about — Bowie County, the DeKalb Independent School District, Texarkana College, and Emergency Services District #6. The play is specificity: a generic benefits pitch invites the response “prove it,” while a number attached to a named school district and fire district is a sentence a supportive neighbor can repeat at a hearing. For any RenUSA client carrying a benefits package into a contested county, the lesson is to localize the dollars to named beneficiaries before the opposition localizes the costs to named victims. (Caveat: Rabbit’s Foot is already under construction with no organized opposition reported, so this is a messaging template, not a contested‑site win — but the framing travels.)
  • Marion County, KSTeeth After the Turbines: a county that has hosted an Orsted wind farm for years showed how to enforce a host‑community agreement after the developer has already built. Rather than venting, county staff walked commissioners through documented, recurring breaches — chronic noise complaints and an aircraft‑detection lighting system that never worked, leaving turbines blinking red all night — and directed the county counselor to draft a formal notice of violation itemizing specific fines and a litigation path. The discipline is in the rule the commission set for itself: “I have no desire to make that threat if we’re not going to follow through on it.” The replicable move for any host jurisdiction is to convert vague dissatisfaction into a documented, enforceable paper trail — complaint logs, contract citations, a dated notice — because a developer answers a fine schedule in a way it never answers a complaint. For our work, it is also a preview of the second act: the engagement that wins approval has to survive into operations, or the host community writes the next chapter for you.
