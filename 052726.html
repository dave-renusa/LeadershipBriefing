Must Read

Ohio’s Thousand-Cut Standard

Yesterday the Ohio Supreme Court vacated the Power Siting Board’s approval of the Oak Run Solar project in Madison County and sent the case back for more process — not on the big questions, but because the board’s approval didn’t sufficiently document the visual impacts of transmission poles and substations. Writing for the majority, Justice Patrick Fischer said local governments failed to prove harm on most grounds, with the visual impact issue as the exception. The project — 800 megawatts on 6,000 acres near Plumwood, which would be Ohio’s largest solar farm — now restarts administrative proceedings with no set timeline. The Power Siting Board had approved it in March 2025. Madison County commissioners and three townships opposed it. Yesterday, they won.

This is how Ohio works now. Before 2021, the Power Siting Board had approved every wind and solar project that came before it. Then Senate Bill 52 allowed local governments to designate parts of their jurisdictions off-limits to utility-scale renewables. Projects already in the application pipeline were supposed to be grandfathered, with local officials getting a voice but not a veto. In practice, unanimous local opposition became a de facto veto. The board began rejecting projects outright — Birch Solar in 2022, Crossroads Solar in March 2026 (a case involving apparently fabricated public comments) — or developers withdrew before facing a likely denial. Open Road Renewables, after getting its most recent Ohio project rejected and previously withdrawing another, announced it is done doing business in Ohio. “It’s become a game of Russian roulette,” the company’s vice president of development said, “and we’re not going to play it anymore.”

Yesterday’s Supreme Court ruling stacks a new procedural layer onto an already hostile process. Even if a developer survives organized local opposition at the board level, the board’s approval can be challenged in court by the same local governments that lost at the board — and Ohio’s courts are now signaling they will require the board to do more documentation, not less. Visual impact studies. Substation renderings. Fire risk analysis for battery storage components. Wildlife and soil assessments. Each is a procedural handle that organized opposition groups have learned to pull, and each can send a project back for another round of administrative proceedings. A project that would move through most states in twelve to eighteen months can spend four years in Ohio’s system and still not have a construction certificate. Research published earlier this year found Ohio leads the nation in both solar project rejections and developer withdrawals among the 19 states that have state-level permitting boards — seven outright rejections and five withdrawals, more than any other state in the study.

For the team: Ohio is not an environment where community engagement work can begin after a permit application is filed. By the time the application lands on the board’s docket, local commissioners have already voted, township trustees have already weighed in, and the opposition infrastructure — organized letter campaigns, yard signs, and in at least one recent case, what appeared to be fabricated public comment filings — is already running. The Power Siting Board’s record since 2021 makes clear it will defer to organized local opposition even when that opposition lacks legal veto power. That means the engagement window that actually matters is the twelve to eighteen months before the application is submitted. In Ohio’s current environment, that window is the only window.

⚡ The Takeaway: The Ohio Supreme Court didn’t change Ohio’s solar policy yesterday. It confirmed it. The visual impact ruling is one more procedural handle on top of a stack of procedural handles, and opposition groups in Ohio know exactly how to use all of them. For any client entering Ohio with a utility-scale solar project, the question isn’t whether organized opposition will form. It’s whether the community engagement work happened early enough and thoroughly enough to make that opposition manageable before the board ever sees the application.
Also On Our Radar
  • The Dubuque Doubling: Dubuque County supervisors voted 2–1 Tuesday to impose a twelve-month moratorium on data centers — extending a proposed 120-day pause to a full year before the meeting was ten minutes old. Supervisor Ann McDonough moved to double the duration: “I do not support data centers and I have said that in the past repeatedly. This piece of paper says four months. It should be one year minimum.” Chair Harley Pothoff joined her; Wayne Kenniker opposed, citing insufficient legal review of the extended timeline. At least 250 people attended in person and virtually; the meeting was described as heated, with shouting, interrupted speakers, and table-pounding. A developer had already contacted the city of Dubuque about water and sewer service for a proposed site south of the city limits — the city confirmed the contact but said no annexation petition had been filed and any connection would require Council approval. County staff now have twelve months to draft zoning regulations, with public town halls to be scheduled. Iowa is in RenUSA’s 23-state footprint.
  • Socorro’s Science Town Revolt: Attendees commandeered a New Mexico Tech town hall May 20 to confront the university’s plan to partner with a Canadian developer on what would be New Mexico’s largest data center — 10,000 acres in Socorro County, paired with a solar array. Residents shouted down the presentation and demanded answers on water use, environmental impact, and the project’s governance structure. More than 4,000 people have signed a petition opposing it. A moratorium vote is expected at the Socorro County Commission’s June 9 meeting; Congressman Gabe Vasquez has publicly called for the pause. New Mexico (NM) is in RenUSA’s 23-state footprint.
  • Springfield’s Rural Spillover: On the heels of Sangamon County approving a $500 million data center in central Illinois, opposition to data center development is spreading from the Chicago suburbs into rural communities as the POWER Act sits in committee with four days left in the legislative session. The bill would ban non-disclosure agreements between data center developers and local governments, require community benefits agreements, mandate water-use permits, and compel developers to fund their own renewable generation — the NDA prohibition being the provision most directly relevant to community engagement practice. An advocacy lobby day is set for Saturday, the day the legislature adjourns. Governor Pritzker has not publicly engaged with the bill.
On the Frontlines
  • OHBack to the Board: The Ohio Supreme Court vacated the Power Siting Board’s approval of Oak Run Solar in Madison County Tuesday, ruling the board must adequately address the visual impacts of transmission infrastructure before re-approving the 800-megawatt, 6,000-acre project — which would be Ohio’s largest. Local commissioners and three townships had opposed it. The court cleared most of their other objections. The project now restarts administrative proceedings; no timeline has been set for the remand process.
  • IADubuque Draws the Line: Dubuque County’s 12-month data center moratorium passed Tuesday in a packed special meeting where the motion to extend the pause from 120 days to a full year came from the floor. A developer had already been in contact with the city of Dubuque about water service before the vote. The planning and zoning commission had unanimously recommended the moratorium the week prior. County staff now have a year to draft zoning rules and hold public town halls.
  • VAThe Homeowners’ Brief: The Oak Valley Homeowners Association filed its opposition brief May 21 asking the Virginia Supreme Court to reject QTS’s petition to revive the Digital Gateway data center project — 37 data centers, 22 million square feet, adjacent to the Manassas battlefield. The core argument: without Prince William County as a party (the county dropped its appeal in April), any ruling would be an advisory opinion with no enforcement mechanism. A three-justice writ panel is expected to hear arguments in late May or early June. QTS is the last developer standing; Compass DataCenters walked away in April.
  • NMTen Thousand Acres of No: Socorro County residents commandeered a New Mexico Tech town hall May 20 to oppose a proposed 10,000-acre data center and solar array backed by a Canadian developer in partnership with the university. Water availability and environmental impact were the primary objections. A moratorium vote is expected at the June 9 commission meeting. Congressman Vasquez has backed the pause.
  • ILFour Days: The Illinois POWER Act adjournment clock is running — the legislature adjourns May 31 and the bill remains in committee with no floor vote scheduled. Opposition has now spread into rural central Illinois as Sangamon County processed a new approval last week. An advocacy lobby day is set for Saturday, the same day the session ends. If the bill dies at adjournment, October veto session is the next window.
  • NCThe Silence Holds: The May 22 comment deadline on the reconsideration petition for Chair Brawley’s unilateral solar freeze has passed. No order has appeared on the North Carolina Utilities Commission public docket as of this morning. The April 23 freeze — issued by one commissioner, without notice, hearing, or evidentiary record — effectively cancels Duke Energy’s entire 2026 solar procurement cycle. Every day without a ruling is another day of operational freeze on 770+ megawatts of planned solar capacity. If reconsideration is denied, litigation is next.
