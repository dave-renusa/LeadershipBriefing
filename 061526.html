Happy Monday, all.

The week opens with a clock running. Tonight the Spokane City Council takes up a one-year data-center moratorium that three of its members introduced only last Wednesday, and the vote is the lead item below because of how it got here: the city did not learn that the 500-megawatt “large load” its utility had been quietly courting was a data center until the public pried it loose. That is the shape of the week ahead — the fight is moving upstream, from “should we permit this project” to “who knew, when did they know, and who pays.” New Mexico gave us the same lesson on Tuesday, when one county passed a yearlong pause and the town next door postponed its own after discovering its moratorium might collide with an agreement it had already signed.

The renewable build, meanwhile, is getting squeezed from the top. The wind industry stopped writing letters and filed suit on Friday, asking a federal court to force the Pentagon to restart the national-security reviews that have frozen roughly $47 billion of onshore wind across 21 states. That is the federal bottleneck we flagged Wednesday, now in a courtroom — details in Radar. Underneath it, the slower grind continues: a township vote here, a county denial there, a 1,500-foot setback hammered out over six months of packed hearings in western Pennsylvania. The map keeps filling in, and the throughline is that permission is now the scarcest input in the energy build.

Four standing items, so we don’t re-litigate them. The New York statewide data-center moratorium: no update — passed by the Legislature, still unsigned on Gov. Hochul’s desk. Must Read

The Pause Goes Inland

The big-city data-center freeze that took Seattle last week reaches the other side of Washington tonight. On June 10, Spokane City Council Members Paul Dillon, Sarah Dixit, and Kate Telis introduced an emergency ordinance imposing an immediate one-year citywide moratorium on building-permit applications for new data centers, and the council is set to act on it Monday night. Mayor Lisa Brown is behind it, the stated rationale is the now-standard trio of utility costs, water use, and noise, and the sponsors were blunt that Spokane simply “does not currently have the necessary framework to site and permit data centers.”

What makes Spokane worth the lead is not the moratorium — those are a dime a dozen now — but the disclosure failure that produced it. The pause follows the revelation that the city’s utility, Avista, had been negotiating with an unnamed developer over a load that would draw 125 megawatts starting in 2029 and ramp toward 500 megawatts by 2032, a figure approaching half of everything Spokane County currently consumes. Avista flagged the deal to shareholders on a May 5 earnings call and signed a nonbinding agreement on May 31, but the public did not learn the mystery customer was a data center until June 9 — two days before the moratorium hit the docket. The council is not reacting to a project. It is reacting to being kept in the dark about one.

That is the part our clients should sit with, because it is the failure mode that is hardest to walk back. A community that opposes a project on the merits can be engaged, offered, negotiated with. A community that concludes it was deliberately not told has moved the argument to a register where setbacks and benefit packages don’t reach, and where a moratorium becomes the cheapest way for elected officials to be seen taking the keys back. New Mexico ran the same play last week. By the time the public knows the load is real, the only fast move left to a council is to stop the clock for everyone.

The Takeaway

The entitlement risk on a big load is now front-loaded into the disclosure, not the hearing. Spokane didn’t freeze a data center; it froze a process that surprised it. For any client carrying a large interconnection — data center, but increasingly storage and even substation-scale solar — the lesson is that the utility’s confidentiality instinct and the community’s right to know are now in open conflict, and the developer eats the cost of resolving it the wrong way. Get in front of the load number with the host community before a reporter does, because the first time a council hears “500 megawatts” should not be the day it also hears “and nobody told us.” Watch tonight’s vote: an emergency designation means it takes effect immediately if it passes.

Also On Our Radar
  • The Wind Freeze Goes to Court: a coalition of nine renewable-energy groups sued the Pentagon Friday over national-security reviews for onshore wind that have been effectively frozen since the spring, and late Thursday filed a motion asking the court to order the Defense Department to resume its ordinary process. This is the bureaucratic bottleneck we flagged Wednesday, now litigated: the plaintiffs — including Renewable Northwest and the Advanced Power Alliance — peg the damage at roughly $47 billion across 21 states and at least 106 projects, backed by an economic analysis from Charles River Associates, and the first quarter of 2026 was the slowest start for new land-based wind since 2018. The new information is the venue. For any wind client, the read is that the clearing-house risk now has a docket number and a timeline, and the relief, if it comes, comes from a judge rather than a permit office — which is both faster and entirely outside the community-engagement lane.
  • Solar as the Approval Currency: a new Brattle Group report argues that if hyperscalers steered part of their renewable spend into community-solar programs, they could anchor enough local generation to cut U.S. household bills by more than $1.5 billion a year, modeling Atlanta, Columbus, Kansas City, and Memphis and finding roughly $1 billion per city could unlock about 1.4 gigawatts of summer peak capacity. The engagement angle is the whole point: the report’s own conclusion is that “the ability to tie new energy demand to local household savings may become a political necessity for gaining project approvals.” That is the benefits case our clients keep trying to make, now with a number attached — localized solar, paired with storage, as the thing a data center buys to lower a neighbor’s bill rather than just its own carbon ledger.
  • Batteries Chase the Buildout: Panasonic said it will invest about $2 billion to chase data-center battery demand, adding a new U.S. production line, expanding capacity in Osaka and Mexico, and targeting a tripling of its data-center energy-storage revenue. The tell for our work is downstream of the press release: every megawatt of AI load that survives a siting fight pulls storage in behind it, and the BESS projects that follow meet the same Moss Landing fire arguments and the same setback ordinances the data center just cleared. The supply chain is betting heavily that the load gets built. The host communities are betting it doesn’t. Our clients live in the gap, and this is a reminder that the storage half of that gap is only getting bigger.
Project Updates
  • TXGov. Greg Abbott, in a Tuesday letter to state regulators, called for sweeping data-center regulation and directed the Public Utility Commission to make data centers pay their own grid-interconnection and infrastructure costs and to act to cut residential transmission costs by July 31. He asked the PUC and ERCOT for a joint memo by July 17 and floated repealing the industry’s sales-tax exemption — a $3.2 billion item — for the 2027 session. Notably absent: any move to give Texas counties zoning authority over the rural, unincorporated sites where most of the buildout is landing. For developers, the “you add the generation, you pay for the wire” principle is the one to watch; for the rural counties our clients work in, the local-control gap stays open.
  • PASouth Strabane Township’s supervisors voted unanimously Tuesday to adopt a data-center ordinance plus a companion noise-and-dust rule, ending six months of packed hearings over the CNX Resources tract on Zediker Station Road. The centerpiece is a 1,500-foot setback from any occupied residence — matched to the township’s existing oil-and-gas pad standard and, per the solicitor, among the largest in any Pennsylvania municipal ordinance — which leaves only about 651 of roughly 1,400 acres usable. The landowner’s attorney pushed for 1,000 feet and lost; residents pushed for a full moratorium and also lost. The board split the difference at the number it could defend by precedent, which is the quiet lesson here: a setback anchored to an existing local rule is far harder to litigate than one pulled from the air.
  • NMSocorro County’s commissioners unanimously adopted a yearlong data-center moratorium Tuesday and launched an advisory committee to draft regulations, after months of opposition to a Canadian developer’s pitch for a data center and 10,000-acre solar array — a project New Mexico Tech had already stepped back from a week earlier. The developer worked the room with a $50 million annual economic-impact figure and lost anyway. Same night, an hour north, Raton postponed its own moratorium after staff warned it might conflict with a memorandum of understanding the city had already signed with a developer — the cautionary tale for any jurisdiction that inks a deal before it writes the rules.
  • MILincoln Township in Berrien County approved a 10-month data-center moratorium Wednesday, on its planning commission’s recommendation, to buy time to study noise, water, and electrical demand before any application arrives. It is one more pin in a Michigan map that now carries pauses in at least nineteen towns — the same pre-emptive, write-the-ordinance-first posture we tracked at the township BESS fights, now applied to data centers before a developer has even knocked.

On the Frontlines
  • MIThe Wire Before the Fight: ITC opened the public-engagement phase on a new 50-mile, 765-kilovolt line from Eaton County to Livingston County — part of MISO’s $21.8 billion regional backbone — with landowner-route open houses set for July, even as opposition simmers from a company already reprimanded last year by state regulators for “lackluster” engagement. Two townships along the route, Williamstown and Meridian, have passed resolutions opposing the line, but because a state Certificate of Public Convenience and Necessity preempts local zoning, an attorney representing 140 affected parcels calls those resolutions “largely symbolic.” His advice to landowners is the playbook worth noting: organize counsel collectively, because leverage on transmission sits almost entirely in the just-compensation fight, not the stop-the-project fight. For our work, it’s the reverse-angle on everything else in this issue — here the regulator can override the locals, so the engagement that matters is the one that happens before the route is even drawn.
  • ILOrchard Beats Array: the Winnebago County Board voted unanimously Thursday night to reject Pivot Energy’s proposed 5-megawatt, 27-acre solar farm next to Curran’s Orchard, a multi-generation agritourism business outside Rockford. The winning argument was not anti-solar — board members went out of their way to say so — it was incompatibility: a six-foot-fenced industrial array next to a place built on families walking the rows. The owner, who spent weeks lobbying, called it a “slam dunk.” The lesson travels past this small parcel: a solar siting case is decided less by megawatts than by what sits over the fence line, and an adjacent use with an emotional constituency — an orchard, a school, a zoo — converts a zoning question into a values question every time. Pick the neighbors before you pick the parcel.
