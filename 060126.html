Volume 10 · Monday, June 1, 2026

Good morning, team.

The week opens with the same lesson the last one closed on: the institution moving fastest is the local board, not the statehouse. Over the holiday weekend a Pennsylvania zoning board denied a 2.6‑million‑square‑foot data center outright; Arizona’s largest proposed data center got cut by roughly 80 percent after sustained opposition; Charlotte is six days from a moratorium vote; and in Central Texas, a coalition of 1,300 landowners asked state regulators to halt a $33 billion transmission line on a procedural defect. None of these turned on a new law. They turned on who showed up, when, and with what record.

Three standing items, so we don’t re‑litigate them: the Illinois POWER Act did not pass before Saturday’s adjournment — but sponsors say it is not dead, and negotiations continue through the summer, with House Energy chair Carol Ammons calling for another subject‑matter hearing. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze is now in its 40th day with no ruling on the reconsideration petition. And Ohio’s Oak Run Solar remains in administrative remand with no timeline set. If any of those breaks, you’ll get a full item; until then, that’s the update.

The renewable build, meanwhile, keeps outrunning the politics — EIA’s latest data has Q1 renewable generation up double digits year over year. We take the longer look this morning at the Texas wire, where the fight is not over whether the grid needs power. It’s over who got told.

Must Read

Notice Not Given

On May 26, several hundred property owners across Central Texas filed a petition with the Public Utility Commission of Texas alleging that roughly 1,300 landowners were never notified of the public input meetings that are supposed to shape the route of the Bell County East to Big Hill 765‑kV transmission line. The applicants, the petition says, added 400 miles of new route links after those June 2025 meetings “without notice to, or any input from, these newly affected landowners.” A companion motion for immediate relief, signed by 179 owners the same day, asks the PUCT to pause the project until the legislature returns in January 2027, to enforce proper notification, to extend intervention deadlines, and to review statewide policy on 765‑kV expansion.

The line is not a private developer’s gambit — it is a state mandate. The Texas Legislature ordered the buildout in 2023 through House Bill 5066 to shore up grid reliability and move power to the Permian Basin; the PUCT handed planning to Oncor and the Lower Colorado River Authority, and the price tag across three major lines runs to an estimated $33 billion, paid by the state. ERCOT’s Permian Basin study pegged the new demand at 11,695 megawatts — 59 percent of it cryptocurrency operations, 22 percent “green hydrogen,” 13 percent other commercial and industrial load, and just 6 percent data centers. If approved, the Bell County East to Big Hill segment brings eminent domain, 18‑story steel towers, and 200‑ to 300‑foot easements through Burnet and Llano counties, both of which have already taken official positions against it and funded legal help.

What makes this one instructive is that the opposition is not arguing the merits — not yet. It is arguing process. The notice failure is the lever, because a defect in who-got-told is far easier to win on than a debate over whether West Texas needs electrons. Burnet County organized early and stayed organized: legal counsel retained last summer, an intergovernmental commission, a protest series, official testimonies filed in April and May, and by this filing at least 25 legislators publicly questioning the project. The PUCT is expected to rule in September. The petitioners don’t need to win the siting case in September; they need the commission to agree that 1,300 people were denied a meeting — and to reset the clock.

The Takeaway

When a linear project changes scope — new route links, new segments, a widened corridor — the affected-landowner list changes with it, and so does the notice obligation. Re‑notice everyone, or the procedural gap becomes the opposition’s strongest argument and its cheapest one. For any client on a multi‑county right‑of‑way, the engagement and the paperwork have to expand the moment the map does. The Texas petitioners are about to test whether a regulator will pause a $33 billion state mandate over a meeting that 1,300 people say they never heard about. That is a community‑engagement failure, litigated.

Also On Our Radar
  • Moratorium Lifted, Fight Not Over: Berkeley County, South Carolina, council voted 3–2 to lift a large‑scale development moratorium, clearing the first procedural step for RWE’s roughly 1,500‑acre, 198‑megawatt Sandy Run Solar facility near Cross. The project still has to clear planning‑commission review and several more readings, and residents who packed the chamber over land use, fire risk, and decommissioning say they will keep fighting it at every one. A reminder that a developer “win” on a moratorium vote is the start of the engagement timeline, not the end of it.
  • Battery Rules, Drafted Local: McHenry County, Illinois, is weighing its first‑ever battery‑storage zoning rules — an eight‑page ordinance with 150‑foot setbacks from occupied buildings and nonparticipating residences, fencing requirements, NFPA 855 decommissioning plans, and Illinois Pollution Control Board sound limits. It is the local counter‑move to the state’s renewable siting preemption: if Springfield sets the floor, counties are writing the fine print on setbacks and safety. Watch the setback numbers — they are quietly becoming the real permitting variable for BESS.
  • California’s Override, Used Again: The California Energy Commission approved Clearway’s 400‑megawatt, 3,200‑megawatt‑hour battery project in eastern Alameda County under the AB 205 Opt‑In program — the third project, about 3,300 megawatts in total, fast‑tracked past most local and regional permitting in a year. The approval carries $1.1 million in community benefit agreements and post‑2026 fire‑code safety conditions. It is the structural counterpoint to every county moratorium in this brief: where locals are building pause buttons, California built a bypass — and the Opt‑In program itself is now the flashpoint.
  • The Build Outruns the Politics: EIA data show U.S. renewable generation rose 11.1 percent year over year in Q1 2026 — utility‑scale solar up 23.9 percent, hydro up 21.9 percent, wind up 2.1 percent — with renewables now at 28.6 percent of total generation and battery capacity up 17.3 gigawatts over the year. EIA projects another 80‑plus gigawatts of new solar, wind, and storage by March 2027, even as fossil and nuclear capacity slips. The point for our work: the pipeline is not slowing, which means the contested‑siting volume isn’t either.
Project Updates
  • KSFinney County commissioners take up Special Use Permit requests this morning (June 1) for Sherlock Solar (400 MW) and Sherlock Energy Storage (400 MW BESS) near Garden City. The SUP does not authorize construction — it sets terms and conditions, with revocation if they’re missed. A clean test of a paired solar‑plus‑storage package moving through a Western Kansas county process.
  • UTIron County adopted a 180‑day moratorium on new applications for data centers, data‑center power plants, and solar plants — but the grandfathered Pronghorn data center, roughly 640 acres near Cedar City, still goes to the Planning Commission on June 4. Water use is the organizing grievance. The moratorium buys the county time to write rules; the June 4 vote tests whether the project that beat the deadline can clear an emboldened board.
  • PAUpper Macungie Township’s zoning hearing board denied Air Products’ 2.6‑million‑square‑foot data center campus on the company’s former 194‑acre headquarters site, ruling on the merits before a standing‑room‑only crowd after refusing a third continuance. A formal written decision is due within 45 days; the applicant then has 30 days to appeal in court. A rare outright local denial of a hyperscale campus.
  • OHLawrence County commissioners are debating whether to assume countywide zoning so they can regulate — or pause — data centers, rather than leave each township to fight alone. Only two of fourteen township boards have sent letters of support so far, and to put a countywide zoning measure on the November ballot it must be filed by August. The procedural clock, not the politics, is the binding constraint here.
  • TXBell County residents plan to pack today’s 1:30 p.m. commissioners court (with a morning courthouse protest) to demand a pause on a Temple data center they say was “pushed through” over public objection. They’re asking the county to intervene where the city would not. Note this is a separate Bell County fight from the 765‑kV transmission case in today’s Must Read — the county is a live front on two energy‑infrastructure issues at once.

On the Frontlines
  • AZEighty Percent Off: Vermaland’s La Osa campus near Eloy — originally 3,300 acres, 59 data centers, two on‑site gas plants, and battery storage — will be cut to 11 data centers and about 1 gigawatt after a wave of Pinal County opposition. At the hearing the board heard roughly 60 comments and 50 emails, all opposed; the developer’s attorney sought a continuance, and the vote slid to August 26. One supervisor abstained, citing too little time to address the public’s concerns. The downsizing happened before the vote, not after — which is the whole point.
  • NCCharlotte’s Six Days: Residents packed Charlotte City Council for a hearing on a proposed 150‑day moratorium on new data centers, with most speakers backing the pause over water, power‑cost, and environmental‑justice concerns while real‑estate interests called the facilities critical infrastructure. The trigger was American Tower’s request to rezone 58 acres in East Charlotte. Council votes June 8 — a major‑metro test of whether the moratorium playbook scales from rural counties into a top‑20 city.
  • UTReferendum Denied, Court Next: Box Elder County’s attorney rejected two referendum applications seeking to overturn approvals tied to the Kevin O’Leary‑backed Stratos data center project, ruling the resolutions administrative and therefore not referable. The county pegs the cost of reversal at $5.4 million a year. Sponsors have ten days to appeal and say they’ll fight in state court. When the ballot box is closed off, litigation becomes the engagement channel of last resort.
  • MIThe Michigan Pile‑Up: Meridian Township, east of Lansing, adopted a six‑month data center and battery‑storage moratorium — this one preemptive, with no proposal pending — joining more than 50 Michigan communities that have enacted pauses across roughly 1,500 square miles. Allendale Township votes June 8 on a one‑year version. The signal for developers: in Michigan, the moratorium is now the default posture, and it’s arriving before the application does.
  • TXTabled, Not Tabled: Tom Green County commissioners tabled a proposed 12‑month moratorium on battery storage, large‑scale generation, and data centers in unincorporated Precinct 4 — after acknowledging Texas law bars counties from imposing such moratoriums in the first place. They rescheduled to give developer Beacon Data Centers time to answer questions. A useful reminder that in Texas, the county “pause” the opposition wants often isn’t a legal option, which reshapes where the engagement fight actually has to happen.
