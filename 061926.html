Volume 18 · Friday, June 19, 2026

Friday morning, team.

The week’s lesson is that the fight has stopped respecting the sector line. We spent the spring watching data-center moratoriums multiply; this week the same energy landed on solar, storage, and the wires. An appeals court in North Carolina upheld a county’s right to reject a 2,360-acre solar farm — the appellate mirror image of Wednesday’s Sangamon story, except here the county won. Battery opposition went mainstream across western Washington, with a dozen jurisdictions reaching for moratoriums on standalone storage. And the highest-voltage fights of the week were literally about high voltage: the Texas PUC hit pause on a 765-kV Permian segment after an organized landowner revolt, while a new coalition in central Virginia worked every Valley Link meeting it could find.

The throughline for our clients is that “renewable” is no longer a shield. Solar, storage, and transmission now draw the same packed hearings, the same emergency moratoriums, and the same courtroom math that data centers do — and the engagement window keeps moving earlier, from the permit hearing to the zoning rewrite to the route-selection meeting that happens before a line is even drawn. The one bright spot for developers came from Council Bluffs, Iowa, where a council actually voted a pause down on “open for business” grounds. It is the exception that proves how rare a clean yes has become.

Four standing items, so we don’t re-litigate them. New York’s statewide data-center moratorium: no update — still unsigned on Gov. Hochul’s desk. The Illinois POWER Act: no update. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: still no ruling on the reconsideration petition. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand. And Wednesday’s Sangamon County buffer-rezoning gambit: no dated outcome posted from this week’s zoning meetings, so we hold it until a real record lands.

Also On Our Radar
  • Pender Holds the Line: the North Carolina Court of Appeals on June 17 upheld Pender County’s denial of a special-use permit for Coastal Pine Solar’s 2,360-acre project, finding the developer never proved the existing Duke Energy 230-kV lines could actually serve the site and never addressed the runoff from clearing some 2,300 acres of timberland. This is the case our solar clients should read alongside Wednesday’s Sangamon denial, because it points the opposite way: a county said no, a developer sued, and the appellate court said the county was within its rights. The lesson is not that boards can deny on a whim — it’s that a denial built on a real evidentiary gap (interconnection, stormwater, the things the applicant is supposed to prove) survives appeal, while one built on a property-value hunch does not. The defense is the same on both sides of the “v.”: close the gaps in the record before the vote, not after.
  • Battery Backlash Goes Big: community opposition to standalone battery storage has spread across western Washington, with at least six jurisdictions — Snoqualmie, Carnation, North Bend, Puyallup, Duvall, and Kitsap County — voting to impose or extend BESS moratoriums in the past month alone, on top of a long list that already paused permitting from King to Skagit to Pierce counties. Developers have quietly pulled roughly a dozen early-stage projects from Puget Sound Energy’s interconnection queue, even as PSE chases a 1,500-MW storage goal and a Jupiter Power 130-MW project near Snoqualmie becomes the first real test of King County’s new siting rules. The fire-and-toxic-smoke argument that stalled Moss Landing has now fully jumped to the Northwest, and it is hitting the storage half of the build hardest. For any client carrying a standalone BESS, the Washington map is the warning: the safety narrative travels faster than the project does.
  • Bluffs Calls the Bluff: the Council Bluffs, Iowa, city council unanimously rejected a proposed yearlong data-center moratorium Monday — one its own mayor had requested — with members warning that a pause would tell developers the city was “closed off.” The candid subplot is infrastructure, not ideology: the public works director told the council the city is nearing wastewater capacity, that a single large center can draw as much as 4,000 homes, and that a new treatment plant could run $200–300 million. So the council chose to keep negotiating project-by-project and write a wastewater plan instead of freezing the pipeline. It’s the rare council going the other way this month, and the reason is instructive — where a town still sees the load as winnable revenue, the engagement conversation is about capacity and cost-allocation, not about whether the project gets to exist at all.
Project Updates
  • GAMilton’s City Council voted unanimously Monday night for a 30-day moratorium on all data-center applications, rezonings, land-disturbance and building permits, business licenses, and certificates of occupancy, with City Attorney Ken Jarrard framing the pause as “a very common sort of thing” for a zoning-empowered local government and leaving the door open to an extension. The tell is how routine this has become in metro Atlanta — a north-Fulton suburb with no specific project on the table reaching for a freeze pre-emptively, to buy time to write a definition before an applicant forces one. Thirty days is short, which usually means a longer extension is already being drafted.
  • NCCumberland County commissioners voted 7-0 on June 15 for a six-month data-center moratorium running through December 15 in the county’s unincorporated areas, after activists packed successive hearings to demand it. The county will spend the pause writing rules on water, power, noise, and proximity to neighborhoods, schools, and Fort Bragg — but the moratorium pointedly does not reach inside Fayetteville’s city limits, where a 225-MW project and a Terra Nexus hyperscale site are already circling. That city-county seam is the thing to watch: a county pause that stops at the municipal line just routes the next application a few miles down the road.
  • VAAmherst County’s Planning Commission gave a first reading to an ordinance that would strip data centers of their “by-right” status and require a special-use permit, after residents traced a freshly cleared lot off Kings Road in Madison Heights to a possible center and took their alarm to the Board of Supervisors. The commission revisits it in July before it heads to the Board, which wants the draft by August. This is the quieter, more durable move than a moratorium — converting an as-of-right use into a discretionary one gives the county a public hearing and a recommendation step on every future application, permanently, rather than a temporary freeze that expires on a clock.

On the Frontlines
  • KSSedgwick Hits Pause on the Sun — Again: the Sedgwick County Commission voted unanimously June 15 for a six-month moratorium on new large-scale solar applications, its second solar freeze in three years, even as two big applications already in the pipeline — including TED Renewables’ roughly 1,400-acre Branch Line project near Clearwater — are allowed to proceed as live case studies of the county’s own rules. That’s the wrinkle worth flagging: the county isn’t stopping the projects in front of it, it’s stopping the next wave while it stress-tests its ordinance against the current one. For a solar developer eyeing south-central Kansas, the window is now — the application on file today is the one that gets graded, and everything behind it waits until December.
  • VABlock Before the Line Is Drawn: Valley Link spent the week running community meetings around Lynchburg for its proposed 100-plus-mile, multi-county transmission line from Campbell County to Spotsylvania, and a brand-new opposition group — “Block Valley Link,” founded by property owner Paul McDonald — has been at every one of them. Three routes are still on the table; the developer says resident input will shape which one it files with Virginia’s State Corporation Commission, which holds the final say. That is exactly the moment that matters on transmission, and both sides know it: once a preferred route goes to the SCC, the fight narrows to just compensation. This is the engagement phase where the map is still soft, and the opposition organized to be in the room for it.
Plays of the Week
  • American Stewards of LibertyTurn the Notice Gap Into a Pause Button: the Texas PUC voted unanimously June 17 to abate the roughly 160-mile Longshore-to-Drill-Hole 765-kV segment of the Permian Basin import build-out — the update to the “Notice Not Given” Texas wire fight we flagged June 1. The play is how the landowner-rights group American Stewards of Liberty got there: rather than fight the line on the merits, it attacked the process, arguing the state’s 180-day HB 5066 schedule leaves newly-noticed landowners as little as 85 days to organize, and it recruited 43 state lawmakers to put that procedural complaint on the record. Commissioners abated the segment to wait for an administrative-law recommendation on a sister segment. The transferable lesson: on a project where you can’t win on policy, a credible “they didn’t give people fair notice” argument — backed by elected officials — can buy a pause that the substantive case never would.
  • Kai Sage / McMinnvilleInform Fast, Then Franchise the Moratorium: in rural Tennessee, where McMinnville and Coffee County each passed data-center moratoriums on June 9 and Warren and Knox counties vote June 22, resident Kai Sage laid out the opposition playbook in plain terms. Her read is that developers target small Tennessee towns precisely because the land is cheap and “a lot of people don’t know what’s going on” — so step one is to alert neighbors fast enough to pack a hearing, and step two, once a local moratorium passes, is to proactively call the next county over and help it copy the template. It’s opposition as a franchise model, and it’s why pauses now jump jurisdiction lines in clusters rather than one at a time. For our clients, the counter is the same speed game in reverse: the host community has to hear the real numbers from you before it hears the alarm from the town that just paused next door.
