Good morning.

We spend most weeks watching communities try to stop projects they don’t want. The deep dive below is the opposite case, and it may be the more instructive one. In Sangamon County, Illinois, the county board voted down a small solar farm that, by its own attorney’s account, it had no legal right to deny — and did it anyway, on the record, daring the developer to sue. That is a different kind of risk than a moratorium. A moratorium buys time; a denial that ignores the lawyer in the room buys a lawsuit, and tells every developer in the state that “you’ll win in court” is no longer a deterrent to a no vote. When the cost of saying no drops to zero, engagement is the only thing left standing between an application and a courtroom.

The data-center pause, meanwhile, kept widening into new ground rather than deepening in old. It reached the California desert (Imperial County took up a moratorium Tuesday), the North Carolina mountains (Asheville), and a Google campus in Indiana (Fort Wayne), even as Spokane — Monday’s lead — stalled out. The national scoreboard caught up to the trend this week too: a new tally puts the value of data-center projects blocked or delayed in the first quarter alone at roughly $130 billion, which is about what the entire opposition movement managed in all of last year. Opposition is no longer a local nuisance to be managed parcel by parcel. It is a market force with a number attached, and it is in Radar.

Four standing items, so we don’t re-litigate them. The New York statewide data-center moratorium: no update — passed by the Legislature, still unsigned on Gov. Hochul’s desk. The Illinois POWER Act: no update. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: still no ruling on the reconsideration petition. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand. If any breaks, you’ll get a full item.

Must Read

Sue Us, Then

The renewable build has its own version of the data-center fight, and it played out in Sangamon County last week in unusually candid terms. On a 15-to-8 vote, the county board rejected Summit Ridge Energy’s “Whisper Walk” project, a 20-acre solar farm on Lenhart Road next to a residential subdivision on the west edge of Springfield — after its own zoning board had recommended approval and its own legal adviser had warned, in plain language, that the denial would not survive a challenge.

That warning is the whole story. Assistant State’s Attorney Joel Benoit told the board that Summit Ridge — a developer with more than 100 solar farms in Illinois — had filed a petition that met the county code and applicable state law, and that “the proponent would have a good case” if the board denied it anyway. He was describing the architecture of Illinois’ 2023 renewable-siting law, Public Act 102-1123, which requires counties to allow commercial, utility-scale solar and wind in areas zoned agricultural or industrial and bars them from writing standards more restrictive than the state’s. The developer had even moved the array about 200 feet back from the road after hearing from neighbors. The board denied it regardless, on the strength of a property-value argument the county’s own zoning administrator conceded it had never actually studied locally.

The more interesting move is what the board is doing around the back. District 7 member Craig Hall has a resolution working through the Zoning and Land Use Committee that would rezone a mile-and-a-half buffer of land around its villages out of agricultural and industrial classification entirely — on the theory, as a lawyer for the supporting townships put it, that “if you rezone it so that agricultural and industrial uses are not part of the zoning process, the solar bill of rights doesn’t apply.” In other words: if the state won’t let you say no to solar on farmland, stop calling it farmland. Benoit isn’t sure that’s legal and has gone looking for outside counsel. The next zoning meetings are tonight, June 18.

The Takeaway

This is the renewable mirror image of the data-center moratorium wave, and it should worry our solar and storage clients more, not less. In the data-center fights, the local board usually has real authority and the developer is negotiating for a yes. Here the board has almost no authority — and is voting no anyway, betting that a denial plus a creative rezoning will cost the developer more in time and legal fees than the project is worth, even if the county ultimately loses. “You’ll prevail in court” is cold comfort to a developer staring at a year of litigation and a hostile rezoning of the ground under the project. The defense isn’t a better brief; it’s getting to the board, the neighbors, and the buffer-zone politics before the vote, because once a board decides it would rather be sued than seen approving, the statute stops doing the work for you.

Also On Our Radar
  • The Wind Freeze, Finally Thawed: a coalition of 18 attorneys general locked in its win against the federal freeze on wind permitting, after the First Circuit dismissed the administration’s appeal on June 15 and left standing December’s ruling that the leasing-and-permitting pause was arbitrary and capricious. This is the bookend to the onshore-wind story we tracked into a courtroom last week: there, the industry is still fighting to restart national-security reviews; here, the broader permitting freeze is now vacated for good, with no further appeal. For any wind client — offshore especially — the federal door that slammed in early 2025 is legally open again, though the calendar pressure from expiring tax credits hasn’t moved. The relief came from judges, not engagement, but it changes what’s buildable.
  • Opposition, With a Number: a new Data Center Watch tally finds organized opposition blocked or delayed roughly 75 data-center projects worth about $130 billion in the first quarter of 2026 alone — a single-quarter figure that roughly equals the movement’s entire output for all of last year, with the count of active local groups doubling to 833 across 49 states. We flag it not for the shock value but because it reframes the conversation our clients keep having with capital: the “community risk” line item is no longer soft or anecdotal. It is now a quantified, quarter-over-quarter drag on the asset class, which means the engagement budget is finally arguing against a number an investor recognizes rather than a vibe a developer dismisses.
Project Updates
  • CAImperial County’s Board of Supervisors took up an urgency ordinance Tuesday for a 45-day data-center moratorium — extendable to nearly eleven months — across its unincorporated areas, paired with a 19-member advisory committee due to report by October 1. The wrinkle for our work: this is “Lithium Valley,” where the data-center boom and the battery-supply boom are landing on the same ground, and the ordinance defines “data center” broadly enough to sweep in battery-energy-storage systems, substations, and gen-tie lines. A storage developer eyeing Imperial should read this pause as aimed partly at them, even though the headline says data centers.
  • INFort Wayne’s City Council voted 8-0 Tuesday to delay, by three weeks, a proposed “Data Center & Community Quality of Life” task force tied to Google’s multibillion-dollar campus on the city’s southeast side — with members saying they wanted more detail on how the body would be picked and run before standing it up. The telling subplot is that the city already has the receipts the task force was meant to produce: officials told the council Google has invested more than $3.5 billion against an $845 million commitment and created 85 jobs averaging about $91,000, and that it’s running 35 neighborhood tours of the campus this month. The lesson cuts our way — the demand for a transparency body persists even where the host has over-delivered, because the issue was never the numbers, it was who got to see them and when.
  • NCAsheville’s staff put a one-year data-center moratorium framework in front of City Council this week, with a vote set for June 26, making it the latest western North Carolina jurisdiction — after Boone, Canton, Woodfin, and Swain and Clay counties — to reach for a pause. The candid part is the rationale: the city attorney says Asheville has no definition of “data center” anywhere in its development ordinance, so it lacks the zoning vocabulary to regulate one even if it wanted to. That’s the quiet driver under a lot of this year’s moratoriums — not opposition to a specific project, but a code that never anticipated the use, and a council buying time to write the definition before an application forces the question.

On the Frontlines
  • WASpokane Pumps the Brakes on Its Own Brake: the emergency data-center moratorium we led with Monday stalled Monday night — the motion to even add it to the agenda failed, with council members saying they couldn’t responsibly vote on critical questions they hadn’t answered yet. The disclosure pressure that produced the ordinance is still very much alive: sponsor Paul Dillon said his office logged about 5,000 messages backing the pause against a single one opposed, and the Spokane Riverkeeper warned that a load this size threatens the river that is the city’s sole-source aquifer. But the procedural miss is the lesson — an emergency ordinance moves at the speed of the answers behind it, and a council that feels rushed will table its own moratorium as readily as it would deny a developer. Avista’s paused deal, and the unanswered “who pays” questions, now carry into the next meeting.
  • NCA Swarm of Small Solar: North Carolina’s environmental agency and a coalition of nonprofits unveiled the next phase of a solar-and-storage microgrid program for the mountains Friday, naming five host sites — libraries, food hubs, community centers, and the Burnsville fire station — for towable “Beehive” units that pair panels with batteries. It’s the renewables counter-story to everything else in this issue: small-scale solar plus storage, sited not over a fight but at the places a community already trusts, sold on disaster resilience after Helene rather than on megawatts or tax base. For our clients, the engagement lesson travels well past the mountains — when solar shows up as the thing that keeps the fire station’s lights and the food bank’s freezers running through a blackout, the siting conversation starts from gratitude instead of suspicion. The host community is the pitch.
