Good morning, all.

Monday’s through‑line was that the local board moves faster than the statehouse. This morning the question is sharper: who actually owns the zoning pen in the first place? In Delaware, the state Supreme Court just answered — the legislature does, even after it has handed that power to a county — and in doing so handed an offshore wind developer a permit a county had denied. Meanwhile, in Tennessee, Ohio, New York, and Michigan, localities are racing the other direction, grabbing the pen with moratoriums before a developer can file. The same instrument, pulled in opposite directions, in the same week.

Three standing items, so we don’t re‑litigate them. The Illinois POWER Act: no update — summer negotiations continue, no movement to report. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: now in its 42nd day, still no ruling on reconsideration. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand, no timeline set. If any breaks, you’ll get a full item.

We take the longer look this morning at the Delaware coast, where a fight that ran through a county council, a near‑collapse of the state budget, and two courts has produced the cleanest statement yet on a question every developer and every opposition coalition is circling: when the local “no” can be overruled from the capitol, where does the engagement fight actually live?

Must Read

Local Control, Overruled

On Tuesday, May 26, the Delaware Supreme Court unanimously upheld a state law that overrode Sussex County’s denial of a land‑use permit for an electrical substation in Dagsboro — the onshore landing point where Baltimore‑based US Wind would run high‑voltage cables from its 114‑turbine offshore wind farm planned near Ocean City, Maryland. The project carries a potential capacity of 1,800 megawatts, enough to power roughly 600,000 homes. Sussex County had said no to the substation in late 2024. The General Assembly said yes anyway in 2025, through Senate Bill 159. Tuesday’s ruling means the state’s yes is the one that counts.

The court’s language is the part worth circulating internally. The justices held that the legislature “controls zoning power, even when it has delegated that power to the counties and municipalities,” and that “it has the power to reclaim it and need not defer to the decisions of subordinate governments.” That is not a narrow procedural win; it is a clean statement that delegated land‑use authority is a loan, not a transfer. Sussex County’s attorney, retired judge Jane Brady, said she was “disappointed” and will not appeal further — but warned that the ruling opens the door to the state overriding other local permit decisions: “I think the General Assembly will do what they did in this case again, because they’ve been told they can do it.”

How it got here is its own lesson in leverage. The opposition was organized and well‑funded — a coalition tied to Worcester County and Ocean City, Maryland, ran a PR campaign through StopOffshoreWind.com urging Delawareans to lobby their council members, leaning on tourism, viewshed, and “foreign investors” framing. They won the county. But when Democrats moved SB 159 to override that win, Senate Republicans had only one piece of leverage — they blocked the state’s billion‑dollar bond bill, which needs a supermajority — and the standoff nearly sank the capital budget before a late‑night compromise let both pass. The county denial, in other words, was always reversible from the floor. It just took a budget hostage to do it.

And the win is not the finish line. US Wind still faces an active federal lawsuit from Ocean City and community groups challenging its federal permits — plaintiffs have already moved for summary judgment — and a harder clock: under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, the project risks losing its federal tax credits unless on‑site construction begins by this July. The state fight is effectively over. The fight the state cannot fix is the one still running.

The Takeaway

Two lessons for our work. First, where a legislature can reclaim delegated zoning, winning the county is not the same as winning the project — the engagement map has to include the statehouse from day one, because a local “no” can be undone by a floor vote, and a local “yes” can be just as fragile. Second, watch where the leverage migrates: once the local permit is overridden, the opposition’s durable levers are the pieces the state can’t touch — the federal permit litigation and the OBBBA construction deadline. When you lose the venue you were fighting in, the fight doesn’t end; it moves. Plan engagement for the venue the decision actually lands in, not the one closest to the community.

Also On Our Radar
  • Seven States, One Refund Fight: New York Attorney General Letitia James, joined by the AGs of Connecticut, Maine, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Rhode Island, and Vermont, sued the Trump administration June 2 in D.C. federal court to overturn the March deal in which Interior paid TotalEnergies $928 million — reimbursing the $795 million lease cost through the federal Judgment Fund — to abandon the roughly one‑million‑home Attentive Energy project 54 miles south of Jones Beach and redirect the money toward oil and gas. The suit alleges violations of the Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act and an illegal use of the Judgment Fund. New York and New Jersey are both in our footprint; the offshore‑wind walk‑away campaign now has organized state‑level pushback.
  • New Maps, Same Fight: Valley Link released revised routes for its $1 billion, 115‑mile, 765‑kilovolt “Joshua Falls to Yeat” line across nine Central Virginia counties, saying fewer than 75 homes now sit within 500 feet of the corridor and none within 150 feet. Lead with what’s new: the redrawn maps are out, a virtual meeting is set for June 10, and nine county open houses run June 15–25 before a formal SCC application this fall. What hasn’t changed — boards in eight of the nine counties oppose the line, Buckingham, Goochland, and Louisa have each authorized up to $250,000 for legal fees, and Block Valley Link (5,000‑plus members) and Preserve Orange Alliance say “the fight is not over.” The route moved; the coalition didn’t.
  • Industrial Only: At Oshtemo Charter Township’s May 27 board meeting near Kalamazoo, residents pressed to confine battery storage to industrial zones, citing lithium‑ion fire risk. The board says an ordinance is coming and has hired an electrical engineering expert to draft NFPA 855 and UL standards. The driver is Michigan’s Public Act 233 preemption: the township is racing to adopt a “compatible renewable energy ordinance” to retain local siting authority before a developer applies and the Michigan PSC steps in. The setback‑and‑zone fine print is, again, where the real BESS permitting variable is being decided.
Project Updates
  • PAPlymouth Township, Montgomery County, set a first public hearing for June 25 on a 2.2‑million‑square‑foot data center on the 66‑acre former Cleveland‑Cliffs steel mill site. The use needs a special exception — the parcel is zoned Heavy Industrial and data centers aren’t a covered use — and the planning commission already voted 4‑0 against it. The hearing venue was moved to a middle school to fit the expected opposition crowd.
  • GASpalding County’s developer pulled back: the applicant for the $6 billion, 2.24‑million‑square‑foot “75 South” data center campus on Tomochichi Road requested a continuance, pushing the Board of Commissioners’ zoning hearing from May 28 to June 25 after the planning commission recommended denial and “Save Rural Spalding” organized against it. A last‑minute continuance after a denial recommendation usually means the applicant is reading the same vote count the opposition is.
  • NMNew Mexico Tech announced it will not pursue Green Data’s proposed 10,000‑acre data center and solar array near Socorro, citing community concerns and insufficient contiguous land. The withdrawal lands as the Socorro County Commission prepares to vote June 9 on a one‑year data‑center moratorium; opposition petitions drew more than 4,000 signatures and nearby Magdalena passed an opposing resolution. The project folded before the moratorium even got a vote.
  • NYThe Clay Town Board, in Onondaga County, took up a new Battery Energy Storage System law and moved to schedule a June 15 public hearing on a temporary moratorium — up to 12 months — on data centers, AI computing, and crypto‑mining. Proposed BESS siting along Wetzel Road, explored by Carson Power and Nexamp, has drawn ongoing public pushback. Upstate New York keeps running the parallel‑tracks playbook: write the BESS rules and stage the data‑center pause in the same session.
  • WVWest Virginia’s DEP set a June 15 public hearing on a stormwater‑discharge permit tied to a 556.5‑acre earth‑disturbance project — a Hope Gas pipeline serving the Nscale/Monarch Compute Campus data center near Point Pleasant in Mason County. The comment period runs through June 25, and the gas‑fired campus has already drawn local organizing and an air‑permit appeal. The permitting fight here is happening one utility connection at a time.
  • TXRCM Hill, LLC filed a federal lawsuit against Hill County over its 3‑2 May 12 moratorium pausing data centers, batteries over 5 megawatts, and power‑generation facilities in unincorporated areas, arguing such moratoriums are illegal under Texas law and asking the court to void it to protect the 1,235‑megawatt “Project Aquila” on 800‑plus acres. As in Tom Green County, the recurring Texas wrinkle is that the county “pause” the opposition wants may not survive a developer’s challenge — which moves the real fight back to the negotiating table.
On the Frontlines
  • MIHeartwood Halted: The Fayette Township Planning Commission, in Hillsdale County, voted Monday night to deny a special use permit for Ranger Power’s 140‑megawatt, roughly 1,350‑acre Heartwood II Solar project after months of opposition from residents, regional politicians, and Hillsdale College. The denial leaned on a newly adopted Compatible Renewable Energy Ordinance; the developer’s next move is a likely appeal to the Michigan PSC under Public Act 233 — the same state‑preemption escape hatch the township BESS fights are trying to close.
  • OHGrove City Hits Pause: Grove City Council passed a one‑year data‑center moratorium 6‑1 after a packed meeting and protesters outside, aimed at a proposed Headwaters Site Development campus. Neighboring Pleasant and Jackson townships have already passed their own pauses. The moratorium wave keeps spreading from rural counties into the inner‑ring suburbs of major metros — Grove City sits just southwest of Columbus.
  • TNTennessee’s Twin Pauses: Two Middle Tennessee towns moved against data centers in the same 48 hours. Cedar Hill, in Robertson County, passed a two‑year moratorium on data centers and crypto‑mining Monday night, with the mayor citing water and environmental concerns and a desire to wait out legal challenges to outright bans elsewhere. The same day, McMinnville, in Warren County, called a special June 3 council meeting on a moratorium amid the proposed 25‑megawatt “Hixson” AI/GPU campus, after more than 2,600 residents signed a petition demanding a pause.
  • TXPowerless in Hood County: Organized residents around Granbury — the “Don’t data center my Hood County” campaign — are fighting eight planned data centers, but county officials say Texas law leaves them little authority over projects in unincorporated areas. Nearly half of the 248 data centers planned across Texas fall in exactly those zones. It’s the mirror image of the Hill County suit above: where developers sue to kill a county pause, here there’s no pause to sue over — and the engagement fight has nowhere local to land.
