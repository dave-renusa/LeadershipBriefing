Volume 20 · Friday, June 26, 2026

Friday morning, team.

The week’s lesson is about venue. The fights we track keep getting decided less by the merits of a project and more by who gets to decide it — and both sides have figured that out. In Ohio, the state siting board killed two utility-scale solar farms in a single meeting, citing not a technical flaw but the “overwhelming public opposition” a coordinated set of local governments put on the record. In North Dakota, the same logic ran the other way: the Public Service Commission approved a contested 92-mile transmission line and told every angry landowner that their concerns belonged to the Legislature, not the docket. Same question — local control versus state authority — opposite answers, one week apart.

And the developers are venue-shopping right back. In Wisconsin, a battery developer that couldn’t get past a town plan commission simply withdrew and refiled with the state utility commission, betting the statehouse is friendlier than the town hall. It is the mirror image of the Ohio opposition’s play, and it is the throughline for our clients this cycle: the most important strategic decision on a contested project is increasingly where the yes-or-no happens, because the forum is now doing more work than the facts. The good news, buried in the noise, is that the build still closes — two big renewable projects flipped to commercial operation this week, in Utah and California, on exactly the rural-host model that works.

Five standing items, so we don’t re-litigate them. New York’s first-in-the-nation statewide data-center moratorium: still unsigned on Gov. Hochul’s desk — no signature, no veto. The Illinois POWER Act: no update. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: still no ruling on the reconsideration petition. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand. And Sangamon County’s buffer-rezoning gambit: no dated, in-window outcome posted, so we continue to hold it. The Data for Progress moratorium poll we led with Wednesday hasn’t moved, so we leave it there. If any breaks, you’ll get a full item.

Also On Our Radar
  • Ohio’s Double No: two western Ohio solar projects went dark in a single Ohio Power Siting Board meeting Wednesday — the board denied Invenergy’s 180-MW, 1,900-acre Sloopy Solar in Clark County, and at the same sitting let RWE withdraw its 116-MW Hillclimber solar-plus-storage project in Champaign County. The tell for our solar clients is the stated reason: not interconnection, not stormwater, but the “unanimous opposition of local governments” and “overwhelming, documented public opposition” the staff said outweighed the project’s benefits. Clark County commissioners had passed a formal anti-project resolution in January and an SB 52 exclusion zone last fall; the siting board simply ratified the local record. The union trades that lined up the jobs called it “a loss of hundreds of local construction jobs” — a reminder that the labor coalition and the opposition coalition now show up to the same hearing, and the opposition is winning the room.
  • Punted to the Statehouse: North Dakota’s Public Service Commission granted a route permit Wednesday for the contested 92-mile, $406 million JETx transmission line from Jamestown to west of Ellendale, after 20-plus hours of testimony and 6,600 pages of record. The transferable part is the commissioners’ framing: setbacks, eminent domain, and even the data-center end-use that worries landowners are “outside the jurisdiction of the commission” and belong to the Legislature, with the PSC’s order asserting state law supersedes the more stringent local township setbacks. Opponents who argued the case to the North Dakota Supreme Court on Tuesday say they “never got our say.” For any client whose renewable build needs new wire, this is the cleaner version of the Permian and Valley Link fights we’ve tracked: when the state holds the pen, the local objection has nowhere to land except an appeal — so the engagement has to happen before the certification record closes, not after.
Project Updates
  • VAChesapeake’s City Council voted 8-0 on June 23 to begin rewriting its solar and battery-storage rules, directing the Planning Commission to bring the zoning ordinance and 2045 comprehensive plan in line with the General Assembly’s 2026 laws (HB711/SB347 on solar permitting, HB891/SB443 making storage a permitted accessory use on solar-permitted parcels) within 60 days. The live stakes: the rewrite lands right on top of East Point Energy’s proposed Mill Stone battery project in Hickory, which the Planning Commission already recommended denying 7-1 on June 10, with the Council’s final vote set for July 21. Note the developer’s pre-emptive sweetener — a binding commitment to bar any data center on the rest of the parcel — a tidy example of using the data-center backlash as leverage for a storage yes.
  • FLDeSoto County commissioners voted unanimously June 23 to direct their attorney to draft a one-year data-center moratorium after nearly three hours of public comment in which not a single resident spoke for the pending project. The pause won’t reach DCIP Group’s already-filed rezoning for an 800-plus-acre gas-powered hyperscale campus — the board cited the quasi-judicial nature of that hearing — but it freezes everything behind it. The instructive crack: the same commission welcomed the project’s first 34-acre phase in March under a “Rapid Response” fast-track, and emails show staff were told to prioritize it. When a CEO can’t tell a reporter whether the site will use zero gallons of water a day or three million, the host community’s trust is the first casualty — and the moratorium is what fills the vacuum.

On the Frontlines
  • UTThe Build Still Closes: rPlus Energies flipped its 400-MW / 1,600-MWh Green River Energy Center in Emery County to commercial operation on June 23 — the largest solar-plus-storage facility in PacifiCorp’s six-state territory, on a 2022 PPA later amended to quadruple the storage. It’s the counter-fact worth keeping in front of the team: built in a rural former coal county, it’s projected to throw off more than $55 million in property taxes for local schools, and the developer paired the ribbon-cutting with $375,000 in student scholarships and a $45,000 gift to the Ferron fire department. That is the host-community template that works — lead with the tax base and the local fire response, not the megawatts — and it’s exactly what the Ohio projects couldn’t assemble in time.
  • CAEight Hours and Counting: REV Renewables and LS Power commissioned Tumbleweed Energy Storage in Kern County on June 23, billed as California’s first eight-hour-duration lithium-ion BESS. The siting lesson rides underneath the engineering one: longer-duration storage is the answer utilities point to when they argue a project earns its place on the grid, and Kern remains one of the few California jurisdictions still saying yes to large storage while the rest of the state — and the Northwest we flagged last week — reaches for moratoriums. Where a county still treats storage as infrastructure rather than hazard, the projects clear; where the fire narrative arrives first, they don’t.
  • IAEight Counties and Counting: Woodbury County supervisors voted unanimously June 23 for a one-year data-center moratorium in unincorporated areas, making at least the eighth Iowa county to hit pause — behind Johnson, Dubuque, Madison, Plymouth, Shelby, Adair, and Clarke. The wrinkle for engagement: the project that triggered it sits on roughly 900 acres of farmland the city of Salix annexed, so the county pause can’t actually reach it, and MidAmerican Energy says it’s marketing the site with no customer signed yet. About 150 people showed at the Salix meeting. It’s the same city-county seam we flagged in Cumberland County, NC — a county freeze that stops at the municipal line just routes the next application a few miles down the road.
Plays of the Week
  • Robin Energy StorageLose the Town, Run for the State: in Wisconsin’s Wheatland, where the town plan commission handed a battery project three unfavorable recommendations in January and the developer pulled it before a board vote, Robin Energy Storage has now refiled the same project with the Wisconsin Public Service Commission — betting the state forum is friendlier than the town hall. The town board fired back June 22 with a resolution begging the Legislature to limit BESS and adopt Kenosha County’s ordinance statewide. The play, and the lesson, cut both ways: when local control turns hostile, developers increasingly skip the town and litigate the venue, and opposition’s counter is to race up to the same state level and try to write the limits before the developer gets there. For our storage clients, the takeaway is to know your forum before you file — and to bring the host community a reason to keep the fight local, because the state docket strips them of standing they value.
  • “Say No to BESS” / Oshtemo TownshipShow Up Even When It’s Pulled: in Oshtemo Township, Michigan, where state law (PA 233) makes it nearly impossible to reject a battery project outright, the opposition group “Say No to BESS” packed the June 25 planning-commission meeting even after the township postponed the ordinance draft it came to fight. Organizer Katie Schneider, whose organic farm abuts NewEdge Renewable Power’s proposed 260-MW site, ran a rally at the farm earlier in the month and keeps neighbors turning out on the theory that “being quiet is not the answer.” The tactic is persistence as pressure: residents say the developer is “following the meetings closely,” so a full room on a night with nothing on the agenda still moves the needle on what the eventual ordinance contains. For a developer, the counter is to be the one who fills the room first — with the setback, fire-response, and decommissioning answers — before “Say No” defines the meeting for you.
