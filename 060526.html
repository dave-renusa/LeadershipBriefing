Volume 12 · Friday, June 5, 2026

Friday morning, team.

The week the local board could not stop saying no ended with the state saying it too. Thursday night the New York Legislature passed what would be the country’s first statewide data‑center moratorium — a twelve‑month pause that a governor still has to sign. The same week, the map split clean down the middle on who gets to decide: Wyoming’s governor signed an order rolling out the welcome mat while Colorado communities bolted the door, and inside our footprint a plan commission in Illinois, a council committee in Michigan, and a commission in Georgia each pressed pause within seventy‑two hours of one another. The pause is no longer a rural‑county reflex. It is becoming the default setting at every level of government at once.

Three standing items, so we don’t re‑litigate them. The Illinois POWER Act: no update — dead for the spring session, summer negotiations continue, nothing to report. North Carolina’s NCUC solar freeze: now into a seventh week, still no ruling on the reconsideration petition. Ohio’s Oak Run Solar: still in OPSB remand, no timeline set. If any breaks, you’ll get a full item.

The renewable build, meanwhile, keeps generating its own fights. This morning’s anchor is a battery‑storage project on Virginia’s Eastern Shore that was three‑quarters built before the opposition found the one lever that mattered — a permit that had quietly expired underneath it.

Also On Our Radar
  • Eastern Shore Stop‑Work: Accomack County’s Board of Zoning Appeals voted 3‑1 Wednesday to uphold a stop‑work order on Scout Storage LLC’s Wattsville battery‑storage facility, halting a project the developer says was already roughly 75 percent built along Chincoteague Road. The lever was not noise or fire risk — it was paper. Scout’s conditional‑use permit dated to 2022 and carried a three‑year expiration that ran out in July 2025; the county later issued a site plan and land‑disturbance permit in March, and opponents argued the underlying CUP was already dead. More than ten residents spoke, all against; the company says it will appeal within days. The lesson for any BESS or solar client carrying an older entitlement: the expiration date on the original permit is a live attack surface, and the opposition is now reading the dockets closely enough to find it.
  • Albany Hits Pause: The New York Legislature passed a one‑year moratorium Thursday night on new large‑scale data centers — the Responsible Data Center Development Act (S10642/A11560), which would be the first statewide freeze in the nation if Gov. Kathy Hochul signs it. It applies to any data center drawing more than 20 megawatts, mandates a local public hearing before construction, orders a statewide environmental‑impact study, and — the part worth circling — requires covered facilities to source a rising share of renewable power, starting at a third by 2030. Hochul has said she’d rather leave the question to municipalities, so a veto is live. New York is in our footprint, and a statewide pause changes the venue map for every project in the queue.
  • Two Bills in One: North Carolina’s Ratepayer Protection Act, Senate Bill 730, cleared a House committee this week carrying two opposite ideas in one vehicle. Part one is a data‑center crackdown the opposition would love: it bars developers from using eminent domain, prohibits local governments from handing them incentives, shields ratepayers from their costs, and requires noise, water, and air site assessments for any facility of 100 megawatts or more. Part two fast‑tracks fossil‑fuel permits, delays coal‑plant retirements, and could scrap Duke Energy’s 2050 carbon‑neutral goal. The bill hit resistance from the Data Center Coalition in the Commerce committee. Watch the threshold and the noise‑study radius — they’re the fine print that will decide how much real leverage communities get.
  • Yes in Wyoming, No in Colorado: Gov. Mark Gordon signed an executive order Tuesday laying out a “Wyoming Way” framework that welcomes data centers while asking state agencies to return guardrail recommendations within 60 days; Cheyenne had already rejected a moratorium, and the Uinta County Planning Commission has recommended approving the Prometheus Hyperscale project near Evanston, with commissioners able to vote as soon as June 16. Two states over, the posture inverts: Denver is one year into a data‑center moratorium, and Larimer and Logan counties have paused too. Same industry, same week, opposite official answers — a clean reminder that the “state” position is whatever the state in question decides, and developers shopping the Mountain West are reading those signals in real time.
Project Updates
  • ILHoffman Estates’ Plan Commission voted 4‑2 Wednesday against rezoning roughly 186 acres at Higgins Road and Route 59 from commercial and traditional‑neighborhood districts to M‑2 Manufacturing for Florida‑based Karis Critical’s proposed third data‑center campus. A nearly three‑hour meeting spilled into the hallway, overwhelmingly opposed on water and rate concerns. Note the through‑line: the same developer was rejected in Naperville before bringing the project here, and ran into the same wall.
  • MIA Flint City Council committee advanced a 12‑month data‑center moratorium 5‑1 Wednesday evening, sending it to the full council. No company has even approached the city — council members framed the pause as preemptive, with the water crisis driving the caution. Flint joins Grand Blanc Township, which passed its own yearlong pause in January, in a county getting its rules written before the first application lands.
  • TXFort Worth residents rallied at City Hall on Tuesday to demand a data‑center moratorium as the council held a 90‑minute work session on zoning, noise, and water rules. The Texas wrinkle is familiar: Councilmember Michael Crain opposes a formal moratorium and argues a “natural” one already exists because projects need state‑level sign‑off before breaking ground. The pressure point is concrete — the Black Mountain site‑plan zoning request is set for June 23, with further zoning debate slated for August 11.

On the Frontlines
  • GAAugusta’s 49 Days: The Augusta Commission imposed a 49‑day pause on new data centers Tuesday after more than two hours of public comment — but pointedly carved out the QTS “Project Eisenhower” campus on Gordon Highway that drew Haynes Station neighbors to the meeting in the first place. Residents who learned a long‑dormant project had quietly changed hands and expanded asked for a full year covering QTS; they got seven weeks covering everything but. A community panel on the QTS project is set for June 24. The shape of this one — a moratorium that exempts the very project that triggered it — is exactly the gap engagement work has to close before the gavel, not after.
  • NYHurley Caps the Battery: The Hurley Town Board set a June 9 public hearing on zoning amendments that would bar large‑scale battery storage — anything above 40 kilowatt‑hours — in direct response to a proposed 250‑megawatt BESS plant at the former Coleman Catholic High School, with officials citing fire and safety. It’s the upstate counterpart to the Accomack fight in today’s Radar: the BESS permitting variable is being set one town ordinance at a time, and the setback‑and‑capacity fine print is where the real decision lives.

Plays of the Week
  • Monterey Park, Calif.The Ballot‑Box Ban: Voters in Monterey Park approved Measure NDC with 86 percent support, making the San Gabriel Valley city the first in the country to ban data centers by public vote. The tactic is the lesson. The city council had already banned data centers by ordinance after a 247,000‑square‑foot proposal drew an outpouring of anger and the developer withdrew — but an ordinance can be undone by a future council, and as organizer Amy Wong put it, a future council can be funded by developers running pro‑data‑center candidates. A voter‑enacted ban can only be reversed by another ballot measure. When an opposition coalition fears the long game — a council flip, a quiet re‑approval, a developer with patience and money — the initiative is the most durable instrument on the board. San Gabriel Valley Progressive Action is already pointing the same playbook at City of Industry.
  • McMinnville, Tenn.Build It to Survive Court: McMinnville’s aldermen passed an 18‑month data‑center moratorium Wednesday after 90 minutes of comment on the proposed 25‑megawatt Hixson campus — and the most instructive move was the one the planning director made on purpose. Sean Garrett steered the city toward a moratorium rather than an outright ban precisely because a ban lost in court could leave McMinnville unable to regulate water, power, or noise at all; the pause, by contrast, is legally defensible runway. And it does real work: the ordinance requires infrastructure, environmental, sound, and utility impact studies as the predicate for whatever rules come next. The play for any community we advise that’s tempted to slam the door: the moratorium isn’t the timid option, it’s the litigation‑proof one — choose the pause over the ban when you want the eventual standards to actually hold.
